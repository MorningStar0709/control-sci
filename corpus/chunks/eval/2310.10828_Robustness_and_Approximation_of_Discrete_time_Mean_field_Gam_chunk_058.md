Now, let us try to summarize what we did so far in this section. Since in case of compact Polish spaces, weak convergence of probability is metrized by the 1-Wasserstein metric, as a consequence of Theorem 4, we see that the mean-field equilibrium of the quantized game has an extension that converges to that of the nominal one. Under the assumptions of the Theorem 4, we can obtain ǫ- near mean-field equilibrium to the original mean-field equilibrium $( \pi _ { * } , \mu _ { * } )$ by picking large enough n such that we can obtain mean-field equilibrium, $( \pi ^ { n } , \mu ^ { n } )$ for the $n ^ { t h }$ quantized problem which satisfies the relations

$$W _ {1} (\pi^ {n} (\cdot | T _ {n} (x _ {n})), \pi_ {*} (\cdot | x)) < \epsilon ,$$

and

$$W _ {1} (\mu^ {n}, \mu_ {*}) < \epsilon ,$$

when n is large enough, where $x _ { n } \to x$ in X. Here the second relation is due to the fact that 1-Wasserstein metric metrizes the weak convergence topology when the space X is compact. We will call $( \pi ^ { n } ( \cdot | T _ { n } ( \cdot ) ) , \mu ^ { n } )$ as an ǫ-near mean-field equilibrium for the nominal model.

We have the following analogue of Theorem 3 in case of quantization:

Corollary 3. Assume the conditions of Theorem 4 holds. Suppose n is sufficiently large so that $n ^ { t h }$ quantized game induces an $\epsilon / 2$ -near mean-field equilibrium to that of the nominal model. Let $( \pi _ { * } , \mu _ { * } )$ be the mean-field equilibrium of the nominal model. Then sufficiently large iteration of the value iteration algorithm of the $n ^ { t h }$ quantized game induces an ǫ-near mean-field equilibrium to the nominal model, i.e. $i f \pi ^ { m , n } ( \cdot | T _ { n } ( \cdot ) )$ is optimal policy for the extended game under the state measure that is obtained as $m ^ { t h }$ iteration of $n ^ { t h }$ quantized game, $\mu ^ { m , n }$ , then

$$W _ {1} (\pi^ {m, n} (\cdot | T _ {n} (x)), \pi_ {*} (\cdot | x)) \leq \epsilon ,$$

and

$$W _ {1} (\mu^ {m, n}, \mu_ {*}) \leq \epsilon ,$$

whenever m and n are sufficiently large.

Proof. Follows from Theorem 4 and Proposition 4.
