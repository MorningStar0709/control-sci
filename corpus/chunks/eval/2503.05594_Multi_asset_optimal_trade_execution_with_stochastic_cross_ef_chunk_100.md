$$
\int_ {(0, T)} \left(D ^ {k} (s -)\right) ^ {\top} d X ^ {k} (s) = T \left( \begin{array}{l l} k & 0 \end{array} \right) \binom {- k} {3 k} = - T k ^ {2} <   0 \tag {C.20}
$$

to the execution costs, where we observe that the trading in the second asset does not enter the costs since the deviation in the second asset is kept at 0. Finally, for all $k \in \mathbb N$ the final trade to close the position is given by

$$\Delta X ^ {k} (T) = \binom{x _ {1} + (1 - T) k}{x _ {2} + (3 T - 1) k}$$

and contributes

$$\left(D ^ {k} (T -)\right) ^ {\top} \Delta X ^ {k} (T) + \frac {1}{2} \left(\Delta X ^ {k} (T)\right) ^ {\top} \Delta X ^ {k} (T) \tag {C.21}= \frac {k ^ {2}}{2} (5 T ^ {2} - 1) + \frac {k}{2} (2 T x _ {1} + 4 T x _ {2}) + \frac {1}{2} (2 x _ {1} ^ {2} + 2 x _ {1} x _ {2} + x _ {2} ^ {2})$$

to the execution costs. If $k \in \mathbb N$ is chosen large enough, then (C.21) is negative due to $\textstyle T < { \frac { 2 } { 5 } }$ . We can moreover choose $k \in \mathbb N$ large enough such that the cost benefit (negative costs) during the time interval (0, T ] overcompensates the costs of the trade at the initial time 0 so that $C ( X ^ { k } ) < 0$ . Furthermore, we have from (C.19), (C.20), and (C.21) that we can produce arbitrarily large negative costs $J ^ { p m } ( X ^ { k } ) = C ( X ^ { k } ) \to - \infty$ as $k \to \infty$ .

In general the existence of an optimal execution strategy is ensured under the assumptions of Corollary 4.7. In this regard, note in particular Assumption 4.3.
