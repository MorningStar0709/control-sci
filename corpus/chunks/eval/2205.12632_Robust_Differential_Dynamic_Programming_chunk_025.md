# B. Approximation by affine linearization

Instead of approximating $\boldsymbol { Q } _ { t } ,$ we can use a linearization of the dynamics $f ( x , u , w )$ , i.e.,

$$f (x, u, w) \approx f _ {t} + A _ {t} \delta_ {t} ^ {x} + B _ {t} ^ {u} \delta_ {t} ^ {u} + B _ {t} ^ {w} \delta_ {t} ^ {w}$$

and a quadratic approximation of the function $f _ { 0 } , \mathrm { i . e . }$ ,

$$
f _ {0} (x, u) \approx \left( \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ \delta_ {t} ^ {x} \\ \delta_ {t} ^ {u} \end{array} \right) ^ {\top} \left( \begin{array}{c c c} r _ {t} ^ {1 1} & r _ {t} ^ {1 2} & r _ {t} ^ {1 3} \\ r _ {t} ^ {2 1} & \boldsymbol {R} _ {t} ^ {2 2} & \boldsymbol {R} _ {t} ^ {2 3} \\ r _ {t} ^ {3 1} & \boldsymbol {R} _ {t} ^ {3 2} & \boldsymbol {R} _ {t} ^ {3 3} \end{array} \right) \left( \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ \delta_ {t} ^ {x} \\ \delta_ {t} ^ {u} \end{array} \right).
$$

For this approximation, we can compute $Q _ { t }$ exactly as

$$
(\star) ^ {\top} \left( \begin{array}{c c c c c} p _ {t + 1} ^ {1 1} & p _ {t + 1} ^ {1 2} & & & \\ p _ {t + 1} ^ {2 1} & \boldsymbol {P} _ {t + 1} ^ {2 2} & & & \\ & & r _ {t} ^ {1 1} & r _ {t} ^ {1 2} & r _ {t} ^ {1 3} \\ & & r _ {t} ^ {2 1} & \boldsymbol {R} _ {t} ^ {2 2} & \boldsymbol {R} _ {t} ^ {2 3} \\ & & r _ {t} ^ {3 1} & \boldsymbol {R} _ {t} ^ {3 2} & \boldsymbol {R} _ {t} ^ {3 3} \end{array} \right) \left( \begin{array}{c c c c} 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ f _ {t} & \boldsymbol {A} _ {t} & \boldsymbol {B} _ {t} ^ {u} & \boldsymbol {B} _ {t} ^ {w} \\ 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \boldsymbol {I} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \boldsymbol {I} & 0 \end{array} \right).
$$

This approximation offers the advantage that $Q _ { t }$ is always positive semi-definite if $\scriptstyle { R _ { t } }$ is positive semi-definite. In addition, we see that for affine linear dynamics and quadratic costs, the matrix $Q _ { t }$ can be computed exactly.
