$$
\left\| \left( \begin{array}{c} \overline {{\alpha}} (\tilde {v} _ {0}, \tilde {v} _ {1}) \\ \overline {{\alpha}} (\tilde {v} _ {1}, \tilde {v} _ {2}) \\ \vdots \\ \overline {{\alpha}} (\tilde {v} _ {T - 2}, \tilde {v} _ {T - 1}) \end{array} \right) \right\| _ {0} + \left\| \left( \begin{array}{c} \overline {{\beta}} (\tilde {v} _ {0}, \tilde {v} _ {1}) \\ \overline {{\beta}} (\tilde {v} _ {1}, \tilde {v} _ {2}) \\ \vdots \\ \overline {{\beta}} (\tilde {v} _ {T - 2}, \tilde {v} _ {T - 1}) \end{array} \right) \right\| _ {0} <   \left\| \left( \begin{array}{c} \overline {{\alpha}} (\overline {{v}} _ {0}, \overline {{v}} _ {1}) \\ \overline {{\alpha}} (\overline {{v}} _ {1}, \overline {{v}} _ {2}) \\ \vdots \\ \overline {{\alpha}} (\overline {{v}} _ {T - 2}, \overline {{v}} _ {T - 1}) \end{array} \right) \right\| _ {0} + \left\| \left( \begin{array}{c} \overline {{\beta}} (\overline {{v}} _ {0}, \overline {{v}} _ {1}) \\ \overline {{\beta}} (\overline {{v}} _ {1}, \overline {{v}} _ {2}) \\ \vdots \\ \overline {{\beta}} (\overline {{v}} _ {T - 2}, \overline {{v}} _ {T - 1}) \end{array} \right) \right\| _ {0}.
$$

Then it must hold that

$$\sum_ {m = 1} ^ {r - 1} \overline {{\alpha}} \big (\tilde {v} _ {m}, \tilde {v} _ {m + 1} \big) + \sum_ {m = 1} ^ {r - 1} \overline {{\beta}} \big (\tilde {v} _ {m}, \tilde {v} _ {m + 1} \big) < \sum_ {m = 1} ^ {r - 1} \overline {{\alpha}} \big (\overline {{v}} _ {m}, \overline {{v}} _ {m + 1} \big) + \sum_ {m = 1} ^ {r - 1} \overline {{\beta}} \big (\overline {{v}} _ {m}, \overline {{v}} _ {m + 1} \big).$$

Thus, $w ( W _ { T } ^ { * } ) > w ( \tilde { W } _ { T } )$ and $W _ { T } ^ { * }$ is not a ξ-hands-off walk, which is a contradiction. The hybrid control sequence, $\left( \overline { \nu } _ { T } , \overline { \mu } _ { T } \right)$ , is, therefore, a maximum hands-off control sequence for the switched system $( 2 . 1 )$ .

The assertion of Theorem 1 follows.
