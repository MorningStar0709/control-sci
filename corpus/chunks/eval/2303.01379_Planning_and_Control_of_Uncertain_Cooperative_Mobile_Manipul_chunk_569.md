We focus on the task specification Φ given as a Linear Temporal Logic (LTL) formula. The basic ingredients of a LTL formula are a set of atomic propositions Ψ and several boolean and temporal operators. LTL formulas are formed according to the following grammar [260]: $\Phi : : = \mathsf { t r u e } \mid a \mid \Phi _ { 1 } \wedge$ $\Phi _ { 2 } \mid \lnot \Phi \mid \bigodot \Phi \mid \Phi _ { 1 } \cup \Phi _ { 2 }$ , where $a \in \Psi , \Phi _ { 1 }$ and $\Phi _ { 2 }$ are LTL formulas and $\bigcirc$ , are the next and until operators, respectively. Definitions of other useful operators like  (always), ♦ (eventually) and $\Rightarrow ( i m p l i c a t i o n )$ are omitted and can be found at [260]. The semantics of LTL are defined over infinite words over $2 ^ { \Psi }$ . Intuitively, an atomic proposition $\psi \in \Psi$ is satisfied on a word $w _ { \tau s } = w _ { 1 } w _ { 2 } w _ { 3 } \ldots$ . if it holds at its first position $w _ { 1 }$ , i.e. $\psi \in w _ { 1 }$ , denoted as $w _ { \tau s } \Vdash \Phi$ . Formula Φ holds true if Φ is satisfied on the word suffix that begins in the next position $w _ { 2 }$ , whereas $\Phi _ { 1 } \cup \Phi _ { 2 }$ states that $\Phi _ { 1 }$ has to be true until $\Phi _ { 2 }$ becomes true. Finally, ♦Φ and Φ holds on $w _ { \tau s }$ eventually and always, respectively. For a full definition of the LTL semantics, the reader is referred to [260].
