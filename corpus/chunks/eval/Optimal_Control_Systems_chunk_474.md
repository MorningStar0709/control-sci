# A.3 Quadratic Forms and Definiteness

Quadratic Forms

Consider the inner product of a real symmetric matrix P and a vector x or the norm of vector x w.r.t. the real symmetric matrix P as

$$
\begin{array}{l} <   \mathbf {x}, \mathbf {P} \mathbf {x} > = \mathbf {x} ^ {\prime} \mathbf {P} \mathbf {x} = | | \mathbf {x} | | _ {\mathbf {P}} \\ = \left[ x _ {1} x _ {2} \dots x _ {n} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{c c c c} p _ {1 1} & p _ {1 2} & \dots & p _ {1 n} \\ p _ {1 2} & p _ {2 2} & \dots & p _ {2 n} \\ \dots & \dots & \dots & \dots \\ p _ {1 n} & p _ {2 n} & \dots & p _ {n n} \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{c} x _ {1} \\ x _ {2} \\ \dots \\ x _ {n} \end{array} \right] \\ = \sum_ {i, j = 1} ^ {n} p _ {i j} x _ {i} x _ {j}. \tag {A.3.1} \\ \end{array}
$$

The scalar quantity $x^{\prime}Px$ is called a quadratic form since it contains quadratic terms such as $x_{1}^{2}p_{11}, x_{1}x_{2}p_{12}, \ldots$ .
