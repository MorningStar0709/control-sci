The guidance and control laws that navigate many of today’s missiles are usually approximations to the proportional navigation law (see Chapter 4). These approximations can have a significant effect on the miss distance, particularly for maneuvering targets. The measurement of all of the target coordinates, such as position and velocity, allows the use of more sophisticated navigation laws. When radar tracking is used, internal thermal noise and external noise, target glint, and scintillation will cause errors in the measured target coordinates that will contribute to the miss distance. For example, at the beginning of an engagement, the thermal and external noise can seriously degrade a missile’s performance by causing erroneous maneuvers that unnecessarily add to the drag on the missile, thus slowing it down. When the signal-to-noise ratio is low, the missile can literally chase the noise. Target glint can also be a serious problem, particularly when the missile gets close to the target, because of the fact that it is inversely proportional to the relative range. Target scintillation is another contributor to the tracking error, and hence the miss distance. This error is independent of range and may be smaller than the larger of the noise and glint errors. Passive IR homing missiles also have angular tracking errors.
