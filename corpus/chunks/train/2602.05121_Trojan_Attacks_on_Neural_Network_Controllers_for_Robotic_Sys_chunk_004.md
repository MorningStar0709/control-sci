# Paper’s contributions

While backdoor attacks have been extensively studied in computer vision and speech recognition, significantly less attention has been given to the security of neural networkbased controllers. Unlike image classification errors, which mainly result in wrong labels, malicious deviations in robot control mean that an attacker intentionally changes the control commands produced by the network, such as altering speeds, steering directions, or stopping the robot when a hidden trigger is present. Because these commands directly influence the robot’s dynamics and feedback loop, such attacks can lead to physical damage, financial loss, or safety risks for nearby humans by violating stability guarantees or safety constraints. In addition, robotic systems often rely on pre-trained models, complex supply chains, and remote software updates, which makes them attractive targets for attackers who aim to insert Trojans into controller networks.

In this work, we investigate the vulnerability of neural controllers to backdoor (Trojan) injection. As a case study, we focus on a differential-drive robot whose navigation policy is learned through behavioural cloning [13] of a classical geometric controller for pose stabilization. We demonstrate how a lightweight secondary neural network, referred to as the Trojan network, can be embedded in the system to silently monitor the robot state and activate malicious behaviour only under highly specific and rare trigger conditions, making them extremely difficult to detect during routine validation or testing.

When activated, the Trojan manipulates the wheel velocities issued by the primary controller, enabling adversarial outcomes such as forced immobilization in critical areas or sudden, unsafe accelerations that disrupt trajectory tracking. Our results show that such attacks can be executed with minimal computational overhead and without degrading nominal control performance, underscoring the feasibility and danger of backdoor attacks on neural controllers.
