Although case (i) is that with which we are closely concerned here, the extension of the possibilities concerning the motion of a body under the gravitational attraction of the Sun should be noted.

It is convenient to interpret the initial conditions in terms of $r _ { i } , V _ { i }$ , and $\gamma _ { i }$ , rather than in terms of $r _ { i } , d r _ { i } / d t$ , and $d \theta _ { i } / d t$ . Here $\gamma _ { i }$ is the initial missile flight-path elevation angle, measured, of course, in the plane of motion, and $V _ { i }$ is the magnitude of the initial velocity vector in inertial space, or relative to the nonrotating Earth. From Figure 6.4, we have

$$\frac {d r _ {i}}{d t} = V _ {i} \sin \gamma_ {i}, \tag {6.23a}r _ {i} \left(\frac {d \theta_ {i}}{d t}\right) = V _ {i} \cos \gamma_ {i}. \tag {6.23b}$$

It is now convenient to introduce a parameter $\Delta _ { o }$ defined by the relationship

$$\Delta_ {o} \equiv r _ {i} V _ {i} ^ {2} / \mu . \tag {6.24}$$

This parameter is termed the initial condition parameter, and it can be interpreted as the ratio of twice the particle’s initial kinetic energy to its initial potential energy.∗ We can now obtain expressions for $h _ { i }$ in terms of $r _ { i } , \Delta _ { o }$ , and $\gamma _ { i }$ . From (6.10) and (6.23) we obtain

$$h _ {i} = r _ {i} ^ {2} \left(\frac {d \theta_ {i}}{d t}\right) = r _ {i} V _ {i} \cos \gamma_ {i}. \tag {6.25}$$

Squaring and introducing (6.24), we have

$$h _ {i} ^ {2} / \mu = r _ {i} \Delta_ {o} \cos^ {2} \gamma_ {i}. \tag {6.26}$$
