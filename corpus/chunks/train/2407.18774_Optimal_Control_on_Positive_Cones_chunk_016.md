where $\bar { F } \in \mathbb { R } ^ { p \times p }$ and $\bar { G } \in \mathbb { R } ^ { p \times q }$ , and

$$
F _ {\mathrm{sd}} = \left[ \begin{array}{c c c c} f _ {1} & f _ {2} & \dots & f _ {m} \\ f _ {m} & f _ {1} & \dots & f _ {m - 1} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ f _ {2} & f _ {3} & \dots & f _ {1} \end{array} \right], G _ {\mathrm{sd}} = \left[ \begin{array}{c c c c} g _ {1} & g _ {2} & \dots & g _ {m} \\ g _ {m} & g _ {1} & \dots & g _ {m - 1} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ g _ {2} & g _ {3} & \dots & g _ {1} \end{array} \right].
$$

Critically for our purposes, the matrices $F _ { \mathrm { s d } }$ and $G _ { \mathrm { s d } }$ are simultaneously diagonalizable. That is, there exists an invertible matrix Q such that both

$$Q ^ {- 1} F _ {\mathrm{sd}} Q \text { and } Q ^ {- 1} G _ {\mathrm{sd}} Q$$

are diagonal. This implies that

$$\left(Q ^ {- 1} \otimes I _ {p}\right) F (Q \otimes I _ {p}) = \operatorname{diag} _ {m} \left(\left(Q ^ {- 1} \otimes I _ {p}\right) F (Q \otimes I _ {p})\right)$$

and

$$\left(Q ^ {- 1} \otimes I _ {p}\right) G (Q \otimes I _ {q}) = \operatorname{diag} _ {m} \left(\left(Q ^ {- 1} \otimes I _ {p}\right) G (Q \otimes I _ {q})\right).$$

It follows from these expressions that for such matrices,

$$
\left[ \begin{array}{c c} X (t) & U _ {1} (t) \\ U _ {1} (t) ^ {\top} & U _ {2} (t) \end{array} \right] \in \mathcal {P} _ {Q} \implies \left[ \begin{array}{c c} X (t + 1) & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{array} \right] \in \mathcal {P} _ {Q},
$$

where

$$
X (t + 1) = \left[ \begin{array}{c c} F & G \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{c c} X (t) & U _ {1} (t) \\ U _ {1} (t) ^ {\top} & U _ {2} (t) \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{c} F ^ {\top} \\ G ^ {\top} \end{array} \right].
$$
