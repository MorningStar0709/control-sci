The errors in the predictions generated by the nonlinear and hybrid models from the testing dataset are shown in Fig. 4. The results show that the error in the predictions generated by the nonlinear model was as high as $3 ~ \mathrm { m / s ^ { 2 } }$ . Such an error in acceleration prediction would be amplified through integration during position prediction and result in large position-tracking errors. In contrast, the hybrid model obtained a much better predictive accuracy by learning implicit aerodynamics, such as wing drag and propeller–wing interactions, which are especially significant during large maneuvers. Thus, the errors in the predictions generated by the hybrid model were less than $1 ~ \mathrm { m / s ^ { 2 } }$ . A proportion of this was due to noise, which might have been sensor measurement noise or disturbances from random turbulent airflow. By providing enough training samples, the NN did not overfit the noisy training data and learnt only the underlying aerodynamic effects.
