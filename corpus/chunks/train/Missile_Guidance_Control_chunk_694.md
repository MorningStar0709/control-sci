An Example Based on the discussion of this section, as well as the discussion of Section $6 . 7$ , assume now that only drag and gravity acting on the endoatmospheric ballistic target are considered. Moreover, let the target missile velocity have a velocity $v _ { t }$ and an initial reentry angle $\gamma _ { i }$ . The downrange of the target is $x _ { t }$ , and the altitude is $y _ { t }$ (assuming an xy coordinate system). The drag force $F _ { D }$ acts in the direction opposite to the velocity vector, and the gravity g always acts in a downward direction. Consequently, if the effect of drag is greater than that of gravity, the target will decelerate. The target reentry angle $y _ { t }$ can be computed using the two inertial xy components of the target velocity as follows (see Figure 6.34):

$$\gamma_ {t} = \tan^ {- 1} (- v _ {t y} / v _ {t x}),$$

where $v _ { t x }$ and $v _ { t y }$ are the velocity components of V in the x and y directions, respectively. The acceleration components of the target in the inertial downrange and altitude directions can be expressed in terms of the ballistic coefficient $\beta$ according to the following equations:

$$
\begin{array}{l} \frac {d v _ {t x}}{d t} = \left(- F _ {D} / m _ {t}\right) \cos \gamma_ {t} = (- q g / \beta) \cos \gamma_ {t}, \\ \frac {d v _ {t y}}{d t} = \left(F _ {D} / m _ {t}\right) \sin \gamma_ {t} - g = (q g / \beta) \sin \gamma_ {t} - g, \\ \end{array}
$$

where

$$v _ {t x}, v _ {t y} = \text { velocity components in the } x, y \text { directions },m _ {t} = \text { mass of the target missile },F _ {D} = \text { drag force },g = \text { gravitational acceleration },q = \text { dynamic pressure },\beta = \text { ballistic coefficient },\gamma_ {t} = \text { reentry angle }.$$
