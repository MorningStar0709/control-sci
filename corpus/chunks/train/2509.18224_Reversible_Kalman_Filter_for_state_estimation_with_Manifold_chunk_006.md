# B. IMU Measurement Model

We consider a standard IMU composed of a gyroscope, an accelerometer, and a magnetometer. The gyroscope provides angular velocity measurements. For a quaternion $q _ { k }$ representing orientation at time step k, the gyroscope yields a 3D vector $\omega _ { k }$ such that

$$q _ {k} ^ {\mathcal {G}} = q _ {k - 1} ^ {\mathcal {G}} \cdot \exp (\omega_ {\mathbf {k}} \cdot d t)$$

and

$$q _ {k} ^ {\mathcal {R}} = \exp (- \omega_ {\mathbf {k}} \cdot d t) \cdot q _ {k - 1} ^ {\mathcal {R}}$$

where $\mathcal { G }$ denotes the global frame and R the relative frame.

The exponential map above provides the link between quaternions and the axis-angle representation of rotations.

A global quaternion enables the transformation of sensor measurements from the relative frame to the global frame. For the accelerometer, this yields

$$q _ {k} ^ {\mathcal {G}} \cdot \mathbf {A} _ {k} ^ {\mathcal {R}} \cdot (q _ {k} ^ {\mathcal {G}}) ^ {- 1} = \mathbf {A} _ {k} ^ {\mathcal {G}}.$$

In the absence of external accelerations, $\mathbf { A } _ { k } ^ { \mathcal { G } }$ is a noisy estimate of the gravity vector.

Similarly, for the magnetometer,

$$q _ {k} ^ {\mathcal {G}} \cdot \mathbf {M} _ {k} ^ {\mathcal {R}} \cdot (q _ {k} ^ {\mathcal {G}}) ^ {- 1} = \mathbf {M} _ {k} ^ {\mathcal {G}},$$

where $\mathbf { M } _ { k } ^ { \mathcal { G } }$ is a measurement of the Earth’s magnetic field.

In practice, the available IMU outputs are $\omega _ { k }$ , $\mathbf { A } _ { k } ^ { \mathcal { R } }$ , and $\mathbf { M } _ { k } ^ { \mathcal { R } }$ .
