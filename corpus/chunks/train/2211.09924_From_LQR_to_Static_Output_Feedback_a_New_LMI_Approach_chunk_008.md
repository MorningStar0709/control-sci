Remark 2.11: If all eigenvalues of $A + B F C$ lie on the left half of the complex plane and $D = 0$ , then the closed-loop system is exponentially stable. From the proof of theorem 3.1 it is clear that this is guaranteed when $\alpha \geq 0$ . Note however that due to the nature of interior point algorithms all the eigenvalues of $A { + } B F C$ might lie on the left half of the complex plane even when $\alpha < 0 .$ , provided α is close enough to zero. Therefore, one should always check the eigenvalues of $A + B F C$ after the design of F is performed by the optimization (27), even when $\alpha < 0$ . ✷

Remark 2.12: Note that F does not have to be the only variable in LMI (27). It is assumed that the matrix $P > 0$ in (27) was previously obtained from the solution of an LQR problem using weighting matrices that will be denoted $Q _ { 0 }$ and $R _ { 0 }$ . However, although the matrix $P$ must be fixed as the solution of the LQR problem, for fixed $R = R _ { 0 }$ the matrix $Q$ can also be considered as a variable in problem (27), provided one adds the constraint $0 \leq Q \leq Q _ { 0 }$ . The reason for this is that if the constraint (27) is satisfied for $Q \leq Q _ { 0 }$ then it is also satisfied for $Q _ { 0 }$ . This adds more free variables to the maximization of α than fixing $Q = Q _ { 0 }$ . Furthermore, it also adds more free variables when compared with the LMI constraint (13) for the case $D = 0$ and is therefore a less conservative condition. Note that since the LMI (27) is linear in $Q$ convexity is still maintained when considering $Q$ as a variable. ✷
