# B. Dual-Input Describing Function

When a relatively slow signal affects the control loop, the steady-state error signal results from a combination of the distorted perturbation—attenuated and phase-shifted—and the high-frequency oscillations [37], [38]. Accordingly, a more general input signal for the calculation of the DFs should consist of a single bias term together with an arbitrary number of independent sinusoidal components [28]. The input to the nonlinear operator will therefore be considered in its general form as

$$\sigma (t) = \sigma_ {0} \cos (\Omega t + \theta) + A \sin (\omega t), \tag {14}$$

that is, a fast component (chattering) in addition to a slow component (bias) resulting from the propagation of a sinusoidal perturbation (3), such that $| \sigma _ { 0 } | ~ < ~ A$ and $\Omega \ll \omega .$ . Importantly, when the sinusoidal components in (14) are independent3—that is, their frequencies are incommensurate—the notion of a relative phase angle loses its physical relevance.

3Independence is defined in terms of frequency: if the sinusoids were harmonically related, their periods would be proportional, introducing a fixed phase relationship. In contrast, incommensurate frequencies (with an irrational ratio) imply no fixed phase relation, making the relative phase irrelevant to the nonlinear response [28].
