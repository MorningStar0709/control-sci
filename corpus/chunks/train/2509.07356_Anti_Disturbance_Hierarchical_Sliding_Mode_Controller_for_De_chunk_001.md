# 1 Introduction

Deep-sea cranes play a key role in subsea construction and offshore lifting tasks[1–3]. Control is difficult due to underactuation and external disturbances [4, 5]. These systems are underactuated, meaning that some degrees of freedom, such as load swing angles, are not directly actuated. This indirect control structure complicates the task of achieving precise load positioning and stabilization in the presence of environmental disturbances.

Because crane systems are inherently under-actuated, scholars have devised control schemes that steer the payload indirectly by modulating the trolley’s motion. Trajectoryplanning techniques, for example, have demonstrated good swing-suppression performance for double-pendulum payloads [6]. Nevertheless, their sensitivity to modelling uncertainties and changing environmental conditions limits their viability for deep-sea lifting tasks [7–9].

Sliding mode control has been widely applied to improve robustness in underactuated systems, such as bridge cranes. Its ability to handle nonlinearities and disturbances makes it a strong candidate for deep-sea crane applications [1, 3]. However, classical SMC methods often suffer from chattering effects, which degrade control performance . To address this limitation, neural network-based adaptive controllers have been proposed, demonstrating enhanced disturbance rejection capabilities and adaptability in marine environments [10–12]. For example, observer-based adaptive fuzzy control has been successfully used for offshore cranes to stabilize payloads under ship deck motions [4, 5]. Additionally, finite-time hierarchical sliding mode controllers have shown effectiveness in addressing unmatched disturbances in underactuated systems [13].

Recent advancements in adaptive control have further improved the robustness of deep-sea crane systems by dynamically compensating for environmental disturbances and parameter uncertainties [14–16]. For example, adaptive neural network controllers with disturbance observers have achieved high tracking accuracy and stability under significant perturbations [11, 17, 18]. These methods leverage real-time learning and adaptation mechanisms, making them particularly suitable for dynamic and uncertain marine environments.
