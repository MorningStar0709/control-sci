Description of the online learning algorithm. We now introduce Algorithm 2 for learning the Kalman filter for state estimation under the informative measurement setting introduced above. To respect the limited access to informative measurements in (38), we leverage a randomly sampled $b _ { i } \stackrel { i . i . d . } { \sim }$ Unif $( \{ 0 , \ldots , \tau - 1 \} )$ ) in line 3 of Algorithm 2, where Unif $( \{ 0 , \ldots , \tau - 1 \} )$ denotes a uniform distribution over the integers in $\{ 0 , \ldots , \tau - 1 \}$ and τ is an input parameter to the algorithm. In a time interval of length τ , Algorithm 2 then queries to obtain $\tilde { x } _ { t }$ once when t mod $\tau = b _ { i }$ , and updates the filter parameter $M _ { t }$ accordingly (lines 6-10). Otherwise, Algorithm 2 keeps the same filter parameter $M _ { t }$ to the next time step $t + 1$ (line 12). Note that the update in line 9 of Algorithm 2 can be viewed as a noisy version of the update in line 5 of Algorithm 1, i.e., a noisy gradient $\nabla \tilde { f } _ { t } ( M _ { t } )$ is used rather than the true gradient $\nabla f _ { t } ( M _ { t } )$ . Finally, note that the for loop from lines 4 to 12 in Algorithm 2 terminates when t reaches $T - 1 ;$ moreover, for any horizon length $T \geq 1$ , Algorithm 2 queries to obtain the information measurement in (38) for at most $\lfloor T / \tau \rfloor$ time steps.

Input: Step sizes $\{\eta_j\}_{j\geq 0}$ , truncated filter length $h$ , parameter $\tau \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 1}$ .

1 Initialize $M_0 = 0$ and $j = 0$ .

2 for $i = 0,1,\ldots$ do

3    Sample $b_i \stackrel{i.i.d.}{\sim} \text{Unif}(\{0,\ldots,\tau - 1\})$ 4    for $t = i\tau,\ldots,(i + 1)\tau - 1$ do

5    Compute $\hat{x}_t(M_t) = \sum_{s=1}^h M_{t-s}y_{t-s}$ 6    if $t \mod \tau = b_i$ then

7    Query to obtain $\tilde{x}_t = x_t + \tilde{v}_t$ 8    Compute $\tilde{f}_t(M_t) = \|\tilde{x}_t - \hat{x}_t(M_t)\|^2$ 9    Update $M_{t+1} = \Pi_{\mathcal{K}_M}(M_t - \eta_j\nabla \tilde{f}_t(M_t))$ 10 $j \leftarrow j + 1$ 11    else
