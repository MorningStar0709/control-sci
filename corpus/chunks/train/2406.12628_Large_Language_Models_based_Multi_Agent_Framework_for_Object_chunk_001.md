# I. INTRODUCTION

Power electronics play a vital role in modern power systems, serving as a critical link between the source of power and the end user [1]. These systems are essential in controlling the conversion and distribution of electrical power, ensuring that power is delivered efficiently and effectively [2]. With the advent of renewable energy sources and the push for more sustainable practices, the importance of power electronics is more pronounced. However, the control methods used in power electronics present several challenges, such as he complexity of the system structure, the uncertainty of system parameters, and the variability of operating conditions [3].

The most common control methods used in power electronics are model-based approaches. These methods generally rely on mathematical models of the system to design effective controllers. While these methods have been widely applied into practices, they often suffer from certain limitations due to inevitable model variations, uncertainties and unmodelled dynamics [4]. Such methods may lead to inefficiencies in the design process. Moreover, the traditional controller design process is often an iterative trial-and-error process. Such process involves a significant amount of modeling, tuning, testing, and debugging iterations, which can be time-consuming and require a substantial amount of expertise and resources. This often leads to lengthy and costly design cycles for practitioners.
