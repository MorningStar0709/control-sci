Recall that $\log |W(j\omega)|$ is positive if $|W(j\omega)| > 1$ and negative if $|W(j\omega)| < 1$ . From Equation 4.24, the net area under the curve $\log |W(j\omega)| f(z_{0}, \omega)$ must be zero; i.e., negative and positive areas must cancel out. In particular, if $|W(j\omega)|$ is much less than 1 ( $\log |W(j\omega)|$ is large and negative) over the frequency region where $f(z_{0}, \omega)$ is large (say, where $\omega = y_{0} \pm x_{0}$ ), a large negative area is accumulated and it is necessary to have large values of $|W(j\omega)|$ at other frequencies to generate an equal positive weighted area. If the weight $f(z_{0}, \omega)$ is small at those other frequencies, then $\log |W(j\omega)|$ must be relatively large over a broad frequency range to accumulate enough positive area. In short, if the frequency band where $|W(j\omega)|$ is small covers most of the range where $f(z_{0}, \omega)$ is large, $|W(j\omega)|$ must be large somewhere else.
