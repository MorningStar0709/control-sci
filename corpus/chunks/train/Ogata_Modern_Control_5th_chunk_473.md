Consider the conformal mapping of constant-s lines (lines $s = \sigma + j \omega$ , where $\sigma$ is constant and v varies) and constant-v lines (lines $s = \sigma + j \omega$ , where v is constant and s varies) in the s plane.The $\sigma = 0$ line (the jv axis) in the s plane maps into the Nyquist plot in the $G ( s )$ plane. The constant-s lines in the s plane map into curves that are similar to the Nyquist plot and are in a sense parallel to the Nyquist plot, as shown in $\mathrm { F i g \mathrm { - } }$ - ure 7–63.The constant-v lines in the s plane map into curves, also shown in Figure 7–63.

Although the shapes of constant-s and constant-v loci in the $G ( s )$ plane and the closeness of approach of the $G ( j \omega )$ locus to the $- 1 + j 0$ point depend on a particular $G ( s )$ , the closeness of approach of the $G ( j \omega )$ locus to the $- 1 + j 0$ point is an indication of the relative stability of a stable system. In general, we may expect that the closer the $G ( j \omega )$ locus is to the $- 1 + j 0$ point, the larger the maximum overshoot is in the step transient response and the longer it takes to damp out.

Consider the two systems shown in Figures $7 - 6 4 ( \mathrm { a } )$ and (b). (In Figure 7–64, the $\times \mathrm { s }$ indicate closed-loop poles.) System (a) is obviously more stable than system (b) because the closed-loop poles of system (a) are located farther left than those of system (b). Figures $7 - 6 5 ( \mathrm { a } )$ and (b) show the conformal mapping of s-plane grids into the $G ( s )$ plane. The closer the closed-loop poles are located to the $j \omega$ axis, the closer the $G ( j \omega )$ locus is to the $- 1 + j 0$ point.
