$$\max \hat {\boldsymbol {\eta}} _ {k} ^ {T} \boldsymbol {\Gamma} _ {k} \hat {\boldsymbol {\eta}} _ {k} \tag {10}\mathrm{s.t.} \quad \| \hat {\boldsymbol {\eta}} _ {k} \| ^ {2} - 1 = 0,$$

where $\hat { \pmb { \eta } } _ { k }$ is the optimal quaternion estimate at time $t _ { k }$ , and

$$\boldsymbol {\Gamma} _ {k} = \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {k} a _ {i} \boldsymbol {\Omega} (\boldsymbol {\omega} _ {1 _ {i}}, \boldsymbol {\omega} _ {2 _ {i}}). \tag {11}$$

Denote the Lagrangian equation of the above constrained quadratic programming as

$$\mathcal {L} = \pmb {\eta} ^ {T} \pmb {\Gamma} _ {k} \pmb {\eta} - \lambda (\pmb {\eta} ^ {T} \pmb {\eta} - 1),$$

where λ is the Lagrangian multiplier. Then, the optimal solution has to satisfy the stationary condition, which leads to the following equation:

$$\frac {\partial \mathcal {L}}{\partial \boldsymbol {\eta}} = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \boldsymbol {\Gamma} _ {k} \hat {\boldsymbol {\eta}} _ {k} - \lambda_ {\max} \hat {\boldsymbol {\eta}} _ {k} = \mathbf {0}. \tag {12}$$

Notice that (12) is equivalent to the characteristic equation of $\Gamma _ { k }$ for the largest eigenvalue $\lambda _ { \operatorname* { m a x } } = \lambda _ { \operatorname* { m a x } } ( \Gamma _ { k } )$ . In other words, the optimal quaternion is the eigenvector of matrix $\Gamma _ { k }$ corresponding to its largest eigenvalue.

One can readily show that instead of summation (11), matrix $\Gamma _ { k }$ can be updated recursively as

$$\boldsymbol {\Gamma} _ {k} = \varrho \boldsymbol {\Gamma} _ {k - 1} + \boldsymbol {\Omega} (\boldsymbol {\omega} _ {1 _ {k}}, \boldsymbol {\omega} _ {2 _ {k}}), \tag {13}$$

where

$$\varrho = e ^ {- \mu h}, \tag {14}$$

and h is the sample interval. Despite the fact that (13) and (11) are equivalent, the recursive formula (13) is advantageous because of its computational efficiency and admitting initial quaternion $\pmb { \eta } _ { 0 }$ , where $\eta _ { 0 } ^ { T } \eta _ { 0 } = 1$ . By inspection, one can show that initial matrix

$$\boldsymbol {\Gamma} _ {0} = \boldsymbol {\eta} _ {0} \boldsymbol {\eta} _ {0} ^ {T},$$

with $\lambda _ { \operatorname* { m a x } } = 1$ , satisfies the eigenvalue equation (12).

In the absence of measurement noise, the optimal solution zeros the cost function (7). However, the estimated quaternion does not necessarily converge to the actual value unless the velocity signals satisfy the persistent excitation condition.
