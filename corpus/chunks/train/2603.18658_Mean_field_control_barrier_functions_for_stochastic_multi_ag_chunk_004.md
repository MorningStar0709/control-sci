# II. MEAN FIELD CONTROL BARRIER FUNCTION FOR ADVECTION-DIFFUSION SYSTEMS

For collections of dynamical systems of the form (1), mean-field models offer more tractable and compact formulations. In this macroscopic setting, the focus shifts from tracking the states of a large number of agents, towards the dynamics of their probability density distribution.

Consider the evolution of a population density $\rho ( t , { \mathbf { x } } )$ in a domain $\Omega \subset \mathbb { R } ^ { d }$ , equipped with boundary conditions that ensure mass conservation, such as periodic or reflective boundaries. In the mean-field limit, the empirical measure of agents governed by (1) converges to the density, $\rho ,$ , which evolves according to the advection–diffusion equation

$$\partial_ {t} \rho (t, \mathbf {x}) + \nabla \cdot (\rho (t, \mathbf {x}) \mathbf {w} (t, \mathbf {x}, \mathbf {u} (t, \mathbf {x}))) = D \Delta \rho (t, \mathbf {x}), \tag {5}$$

where w denotes a velocity field parameterized by a Lipschitz continuous control input u, and $D > 0$ is the diffusion coefficient. Here, we operate within mean-field control/meanfield game frameworks, where the collective behavior of large populations of agents is modeled and controlled at the level of density distributions [17]–[19].

Let $\mathcal { H }$ be a continuously differentiable functional on the space of densities, and define the associated safe set $\mathcal { C }$ as

$$\mathcal {C} = \{\rho : \mathcal {H} (t, \rho) > 0 \}, \quad \partial \mathcal {C} = \{\rho : \mathcal {H} (t, \rho) = 0 \}. \tag {6}$$

Following the MF-CBF paradigm, assuming $\mathcal { H } ( t _ { 0 } , \rho ) \ge 0$ at initial time $t _ { 0 } ,$ forward invariance of $\mathcal { C }$ is ensured by enforcing the mean-field control barrier condition

$$\frac {\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d} t} \mathcal {H} (t, \boldsymbol {\rho} (t, \mathbf {x})) \geq - \alpha (\mathcal {H} (t, \boldsymbol {\rho} (t, \mathbf {x}))), \tag {7}$$

where $\alpha ( \cdot )$ is a class- $\mathcal { H }$ function. The explicit time dependence of H allows this formulation to accommodate timevarying (e.g., moving) dangerous zones.
