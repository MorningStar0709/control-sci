The transmission of the constant level b and of the first harmonic $\sin\omega t$ are thus characterized by the equivalent gains $N_{B}$ and $N_{A}$ . Since the linear parts will normally attenuate high frequencies more than low frequencies, a reasonable approximation is often obtained by considering only the constant part and the first harmonic. The number $N_{B}$ , which describes the propagation of a constant signal, is called the dual-input describing function, by analogy with the ordinary describing function that describes the propagation of sinusoids through static nonlinearities. Notice that the describing function $N_{B}$ depends on a (the amplitude of the sinusoidal oscillation). This dependence is the key to understanding how the SOAS works. The dual-input describing function can be used to characterize the transmission of slowly varying signals. A detailed analysis of the accuracy of the approximation is fairly complicated. Let it therefore suffice to mention some rules of thumb for using the approximation. The ratio a/b should be greater than 3, and the ratio of the limit cycle frequency to the signal frequency should also be greater than 3. It is strongly recommended that the analysis be supplemented by simulation.
