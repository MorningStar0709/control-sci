The root locus in Fig. 10.38 shows that the closed-loop system remains stable for all gains $K _ { P } > 0$ , as the loci never cross the imaginary axis into the right-half plane. Furthermore, the control engineer can adjust the gain $K _ { P }$ and obtain a wide range of undamped natural frequency $\omega _ { n }$ and damping ratio $\zeta$ for the two complex poles. The MATLAB command sgrid can be used to overlay grid lines of constant $\omega _ { n }$ and $\zeta$ on an existing root locus, as shown in Fig. 10.39. Figure 10.39 presents the same root locus as shown in Fig. 10.38 but with semicircular grid lines of constant $\omega _ { n }$ and radial grid lines of constant $\zeta$ (recall that the distance from the origin to the root is $\omega _ { n }$ and that $\zeta$ decreases as the radial line from the origin to the root approaches the imaginary axis). The radia $\zeta$ lines in Fig. 10.39 show that closed-loop damping ratio is always greater than 0.5 for all complex roots. Furthermore, it is possible to select a gain $K _ { P }$ such that the closed-loop complex roots simultaneously have a fast response $( \omega _ { n } \mathrm { ~ > ~ } 1 0 \mathrm { r a d / s ) }$ with very good damping $( \zeta ~ > ~ 0 . 9 4 )$ ). This “wedge” location in the root locus for fast response and good damping is indicated in Fig. 10.39. In summary, the root locus in Fig. 10.39 shows that a simple P-controller can provide a desirable closed-loop transient response for the system shown in Fig. 10.37.
