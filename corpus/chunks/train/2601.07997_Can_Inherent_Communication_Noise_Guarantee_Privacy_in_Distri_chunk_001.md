# 1. INTRODUCTION

In recent years, distributed cooperative control has become a highly active research area owing to its broad applications in mobile robotics, unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs), and social networks (Li et al., 2018; Cao et al., 2025; D’Alfonso and Fedele, 2025). A significant number of research results have been reported (Oh et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2022). The primary objective of distributed cooperative control is to drive a group of autonomous agents to achieve common goals or tasks through information exchange among neighbouring agents. However, the data shared among agents may give rise to potential privacy leakage, as external eavesdroppers can extract sensitive information therein, such as the reference signal, control preferences, and individual system dynamics. In fact, it has been proven that in the absence of privacy-preserving mechanisms, an eavesdropper can reconstruct the training data from shared state information (Wang and Nedi´c, 2023).

To address privacy concerns, differential privacy (DP) provides a rigorous framework to quantify and manage the trade-off between privacy and performance (Dwork et al., 2006, 2014). Compared with other privacy-preserving techniques, DP is attractive as it is immune to post-processing, robust against side information, and provides a quantifiable and tunable level of privacy protection. Moreover, as systems evolve toward tighter human–machine integration within cyber-physical-human systems (Annaswamy et al., 2023), DP offers a principled mechanism that enables users to interactively adjust system behavior while maintaining formal privacy guarantees.
