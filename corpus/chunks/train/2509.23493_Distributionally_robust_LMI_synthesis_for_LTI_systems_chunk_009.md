At this point, we notice that (5) is a quadratic optimal control problem over random variables, as studied in [5]. In [5], this problem is approached with the averaged second moment matrices $\Sigma ( T )$ , which are defined to be equal to

$$
\left( \begin{array}{c c c} \Sigma_ {\chi \chi} (T) & \Sigma_ {\chi w} (T) & \Sigma_ {\chi \hat {w}} (T) \\ \Sigma_ {w \chi} (T) & \Sigma_ {w w} (T) & \Sigma_ {w \hat {w}} (T) \\ \Sigma_ {\hat {w} \chi} (T) & \Sigma_ {\hat {w} w} (T) & \Sigma_ {\hat {w} \hat {w}} (T) \end{array} \right) := \frac {1}{T} \sum_ {t = 0} ^ {T - 1} \mathbb {E} \left( \begin{array}{c} \chi (t) \\ w (t) \\ \hat {w} (t) \end{array} \right) \left( \begin{array}{c} \chi (t) \\ w (t) \\ \hat {w} (t) \end{array} \right) ^ {\top},
$$

and a stationary moment matrix Σ, where the latter is supposed to represent the limit of $\Sigma ( T )$ as $T \to \infty$ if that limit exists. Using the stationary moment matrix Σ, we define the moment relaxed optimization problem

$$
\underset {\Sigma \in \mathcal {S}} {\text { maximize }} \quad \operatorname{tr} \left(\left( \begin{array}{c c c} \mathcal {C} & \mathcal {D} & 0 \end{array} \right) \Sigma \left( \begin{array}{c c c} \mathcal {C} & \mathcal {D} & 0 \end{array} \right) ^ {\top}\right), \tag {6a}
$$

subject to

$$
\left( \begin{array}{c c c} I & 0 & 0 \end{array} \right) \Sigma \left( \begin{array}{c c c} I & 0 & 0 \end{array} \right) ^ {\top} = \left( \begin{array}{c c c} \mathcal {A} & \mathcal {B} & 0 \end{array} \right) \Sigma \left( \begin{array}{c c c} \mathcal {A} & \mathcal {B} & 0 \end{array} \right) ^ {\top}, \tag {6b}
