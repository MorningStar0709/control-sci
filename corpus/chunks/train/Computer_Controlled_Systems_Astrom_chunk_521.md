# 8.6 Conclusions

Different ways of translating a continuous-time controller to a digital controller have been presented. The problem is of substantial interest if an analog design is available, and a digital solution is needed. Several methods to compute a pulse-transfer function that corresponds to the continuous-time transfer function have been discussed, based on step invariance, ramp invariance, and Tustin's approximation. Tustin's method is commonly used because of its simplicity. It does, however, distort the frequency scale of the filter. The method based on ramp invariance gives very good results and is only moderately more complicated than Tustin's method. Digital systems designed in this way are always (slightly) inferior to analog systems because of the inherent time delay caused by the hold circuit. This time delay is approximately h/2.

The translation methods work well if the sampling period is short. A good way to choose the sampling period is to observe that the extra time delay decreases the phase margin by $\omega_{c}h/2$ radians or by $180\omega_{c}/\omega_{s}$ degrees, where $\omega_{c}$ is the crossover frequency. There are possibilities of creating better designs than those discussed in this chapter, as discussed in the following chapters.
