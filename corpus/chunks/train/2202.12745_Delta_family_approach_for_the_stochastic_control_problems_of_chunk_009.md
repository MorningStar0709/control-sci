# 2.2 Delta family algorithm for the stochastic control problems

Consider a general 1-D stochastic control problem with its value function characterized by

$$V (t, x) = \sup _ {\pi \in \mathcal {A}} \mathbb {E} [ U (X _ {T}) \mid X _ {t} = x ],$$

where U represents the utility function that is continuous on $[ 0 , + \infty )$ and satisfies $U ( x ) =$ −∞ for $x < 0$ , and $U ( x ) \leq C ( 1 + x ^ { \varrho } )$ for some constants $C > 0$ and $0 < \varrho < 1$ . The process $X _ { t }$ depends on the unknown control π. From Bellman’s dynamical programming principle, we have the following characterization of the value function:

$$V (t, x) = \sup _ {\pi \in \mathcal {A}} \mathbb {E} [ V (t + h, X _ {t + h}) \mid X _ {t} = x ]. \tag {2.6}$$

Based on (2.6), the traditional approach proceeds as follows:

1. Assume $V ( t , x ) \in C ^ { 1 , 2 }$ , and then apply the Ito’s lemma to rewrite the right hand side of (2.6) into a differential form.   
2. Then take the limit $h  0$ to characterize the local behavior of the value function. This is achieved through obtaining the HJB PDE, which is a highly nonlinear PDE and challenging to solve.

We shall take an alternative approach, and the idea is to rewrite the conditional expectation in (2.6) using the conditional transition density function, and then use our previous representation in (2.3) to represent the density function. Note that the unknown control is only associated with the conditional density function. We have

$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathbb {E} [ V (t + h, X _ {t + h}) \mid X _ {t} = x ] = \int_ {\mathbb {R} _ {+}} V (t + h, y) f (y \mid x) d y \\ = \sum_ {k = 0} ^ {\infty} \left(\int_ {\mathbb {R} _ {+}} V (t + h, y) g _ {k} (y) d y\right) \mathbb {E} _ {x} \left[ g _ {k} \left(X _ {t + h}\right) \right] \\ = \sum_ {k = 0} ^ {\infty} \left(\int_ {\mathbb {R} _ {+}} V (t + h, y) g _ {k} (y) d y\right) \left(\int_ {t} ^ {t + h} \mathbb {E} _ {x} [ \mathcal {L} ^ {\pi} g _ {k} (X _ {u}) ] d u + g _ {k} (x)\right), \tag {2.7} \\ \end{array}
$$

where ${ \mathcal { L } } ^ { \pi }$ is the infinitesimal generator of the Markov process X, and note that it contains the unknown control. Plugging (2.7) into (2.6), we have

$$
\begin{array}{l} V (t, x) = \sup _ {\pi \in \mathcal {A}} \mathbb {E} [ V (t + h, X _ {t + h}) \mid X _ {t} = x ] \\ = \sup _ {\pi \in \mathcal {A}} \sum_ {k = 0} ^ {\infty} \left(\int_ {\mathbb {R} _ {+}} V (t + h, y) g _ {k} (y) d y\right) \left(\int_ {t} ^ {t + h} \mathbb {E} _ {x} [ \mathcal {L} ^ {\pi} g _ {k} (X _ {u}) ] d u + g _ {k} (x)\right). \tag {2.8} \\ \end{array}
$$
