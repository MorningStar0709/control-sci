$$
\boldsymbol {Z} = \left[ \begin{array}{l} \boldsymbol {z} _ {1} \\ \boldsymbol {z} _ {2} \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array}{l} \boldsymbol {\xi} _ {1} - \boldsymbol {\xi} _ {1 d} \\ \boldsymbol {\xi} _ {2} - \boldsymbol {\xi} _ {2 d} \end{array} \right]. \tag {6}
$$

Denoting the reference state as $\pmb { \xi } _ { d } = \left[ \pmb { \xi } _ { 1 d } , \pmb { \xi } _ { 2 d } \right] ^ { T }$ , the aim of the LNMPC is to find optimal control signal U to drive the state variable $\boldsymbol { \xi }$ to the desired state $\xi _ { d }$ . This can be carried out by optimizing a cost function with constraints of the form

$$J = \min _ {\boldsymbol {U}} \| \boldsymbol {\xi} (t _ {k} + T) - \boldsymbol {\xi} _ {d} (t _ {k} + T) \| _ {\boldsymbol {P}} ^ {2} + \int_ {t _ {k}} ^ {t _ {k} + T} \left(\| \boldsymbol {\xi} (t) - \boldsymbol {\xi} _ {d} (t) \| _ {\boldsymbol {Q}} ^ {2} + \| \boldsymbol {U} (t) \| _ {\boldsymbol {R}} ^ {2}\right) d t \tag {7}$$

subject to

$$\boldsymbol {\xi} \left(t _ {k}\right) = \boldsymbol {\xi} \left(t = t _ {k}\right) \tag {8a}\dot {\boldsymbol {\xi}} (t) = f (\boldsymbol {\xi} (t), \boldsymbol {U} (t)) \forall t \in [ t _ {k}; t _ {k} + T ] \tag {8b}| \boldsymbol {\xi} (t) | \leq \boldsymbol {\xi} _ {m a x} \forall t \in [ t _ {k}; t _ {k} + T ] \tag {8c}\left| \boldsymbol {U} (t) \right| \leq \boldsymbol {U} _ {\max} \forall t \in \left[ t _ {k}; t _ {k} + T \right] \tag {8d}\frac {\partial V (\boldsymbol {\xi})}{\partial \boldsymbol {\xi}} f \left(\boldsymbol {\xi} \left(t _ {k}\right), \boldsymbol {U} \left(t _ {k}\right)\right) \leq \frac {\partial V (\boldsymbol {\xi})}{\partial \boldsymbol {\xi}} f \left(\boldsymbol {\xi} \left(t _ {k}\right), h \left(\boldsymbol {\xi} \left(t _ {k}\right)\right)\right), \tag {8e}$$

where $t _ { k }$ is the current time instant, $T$ is the prediction horizon length, $U \left( t \right)$ is the prediction control signal at future time $t , \| \cdot \|$ denotes the Euclidean norm, $P , Q$ , and R are the positive definite weight matrices, $h \left( \cdot \right)$ is a Lyapunov-based closed-loop control law, and $V \left( \cdot \right)$ is a Lyapunov function.
