# 4.1 Effect of Stochastic Disturbances

Consider the estimation of a plant model disturbed by a stochastic process which can be represented by:

$$
\begin{array}{l} y (t + 1) = - A ^ {*} (q ^ {- 1}) y (t) + B ^ {*} (q ^ {- 1}) u (t) + w (t + 1) \\ = \theta^ {T} \phi (t) + w (t + 1) \tag {4.1} \\ \end{array}
$$

where u is the input, y is the output, w is a zero mean stationary stochastic disturbance with finite moments and:

$$\theta^ {T} = \left[ a _ {1}, \dots , a _ {n _ {A}}, b _ {1}, \dots , b _ {n _ {B}} \right] \tag {4.2}\phi^ {T} (t) = [ - y (t), \ldots , - y (t - n _ {A} + 1), u (t), \ldots , u (t - n _ {B} + 1) ] \qquad (4. 3)$$

Let’s consider an equation error type adjustable predictor (like in RLS). In this case, the a posteriori output of the adjustable predictor is given by:

$$\hat {y} (t + 1) = \hat {\theta} ^ {T} (t + 1) \phi (t) \tag {4.4}$$

where:

$$\hat {\theta} ^ {T} (t) = [ \hat {a} _ {1} (t), \dots , \hat {a} _ {n _ {A}} (t), \hat {b} _ {1} (t), \dots , \hat {b} _ {n _ {B}} (t) ] \tag {4.5}$$

The a posteriori prediction error is characterized by the following equation:

$$\varepsilon (t + 1) = y (t + 1) - \hat {y} (t + 1) = [ \theta - \hat {\theta} (t + 1) ] ^ {T} \phi (t) + w (t + 1) \tag {4.6}$$

and the PAA will have the form:

$$\hat {\theta} (t + 1) = \hat {\theta} (t) + F (t) \phi (t) \varepsilon (t + 1) \tag {4.7}$$

The first remark to be made is that in the presence of a zero mean stochastic disturbance, even if $\hat { \theta } ( t ) \equiv \theta$ , the prediction error will not be null. Therefore, if we want that the estimated parameter vector ${ \hat { \theta } } ( t )$ tends toward a constant value, the adaptation gain $F ( t )$ in (4.7) should tend toward zero when $t \to \infty$ (vanishing adaptation gain).

This means that in the case of a time-varying matrix adaptation gain updated by:

$$F (t + 1) ^ {- 1} = \lambda_ {1} (t) F (t) ^ {- 1} + \lambda_ {2} (t) \phi (t) \phi^ {T} (t) \tag {4.8}$$

$\lambda _ { 1 } ( t )$ and $\lambda _ { 2 } ( t )$ should be chosen accordingly, i.e.:

$$0 < \lambda_ {2} (t) < 2 \tag {4.9}$$

and the possible choices for $\lambda _ { 1 } ( t )$ are:

$$
\begin{array}{l} \left. \begin{array}{l l} (\mathrm{a}) & \lambda_ {1} (t) \equiv 1 \\ (\mathrm{b}) & \lambda_ {1} (t) = 1 \quad \text { for } t \geq t _ {0} \end{array} \right\} \tag {4.10} \\ \text {(c)} \quad \lim _ {t \to \infty} \lambda_ {1} (t) = 1 \\ \end{array}
$$

Similar reasoning applies when F (t) is of the form:

$$F (t) = \frac {1}{p (t)} \tag {4.11}$$
