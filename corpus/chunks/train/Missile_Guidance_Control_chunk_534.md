It follows that if a closed path intersects the Earth, the perigee must be interior to the Earth, as shown in Figure 6.9. One of the most significant design parameters of a ballistic missile is the range covered over the surface of the Earth. From Figure 6.9, it is clear that this quantity, from the initial instant to any other time, is simply

$$R = r _ {e} (\nu - \nu_ {i}), \tag {6.65}$$

where the true anomaly ν is related to the time t through (6.38). Using (6.22b) and (6.26), we have $( \nu - \nu _ { i } ) = \theta$ , and so

$$R = r _ {e} \theta . \tag {6.66}$$

The angle θ may be obtained by solving (6.21). Or in terms of Figure 6.9, the range is $r _ { e } \sigma$ . Therefore, of interest here is the determination of the range $r _ { e } \sigma$ , the height $H _ { m a x }$ , and the time $t = T$ as a function of the initial conditions, which are ${ r } _ { i } = { r } _ { e } , { V } _ { i }$ , and $\gamma _ { i }$ . The eccentricity is determined from the equation

$$e ^ {2} = [ (R V _ {i} ^ {2} / \mu) - 1 ] ^ {2} \cos^ {2} \gamma_ {i} + \sin^ {2} \gamma_ {i}.$$

Consequently, the range of the missile in free flight, that is, the range as it travels from the initial point (or burnout) on the reference sphere to the apogee and back to the reference sphere (at the terminal or reentry point) can be defined in terms of the geocentric angle $\sigma$ by the expression

$$\sigma = 2 (\pi - \nu), \tag {6.67}$$

where ν is the true anomaly. Using the orbit equation (6.21), the range may be related to the orbit parameters as follows:

$$r _ {i} = \left(h ^ {2} / \mu\right) / (1 + e \cos \nu) = \left(h ^ {2} / \mu\right) [ 1 + e \cos (\pi - (\sigma / 2)) ]. \tag {6.68}$$

From Figure 6.9, the altitude at the apogee, that is, $H _ { m a x }$ , is given by the expression

$$H _ {m a x} = r _ {a} - r _ {e} = (h ^ {2} / \mu) (1 - e) - r _ {e}.$$
