$$A (\alpha) = \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {n} A ^ {(i)} \mathbf {1} _ {\{\alpha = i \}}, \tag {7}B (\alpha) = \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {n} B ^ {(i)} \mathbf {1} _ {\{\alpha = i \}}, \tag {8}$$

where $\mathbf { 1 } _ { \{ \alpha = i \} }$ equals 1 when $\alpha = i ,$ and 0 otherwise.

2. Measurement Contingencies: These affect only the output measurement matrix $C ( \alpha )$ , such as sensor failures or spoofing attacks, while the system dynamics remain unchanged. This leads to:

$$C (\alpha) = \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {m} C ^ {(i)} \mathbf {1} _ {\{\alpha = i \}}. \tag {9}$$

To assess the observability of the system under different scenarios, we use the observability matrix:

$$\mathcal {O} (\alpha) = \left[ C (\alpha) ^ {T} \quad (C (\alpha) A (\alpha)) ^ {T} \dots (C (\alpha) A (\alpha) ^ {n - 1}) ^ {T} \right] ^ {T}. \tag {10}$$

Since multiple contingencies may yield systems with similar structures and overlapping eigenvalues, it becomes necessary to design inputs that help distinguish between these scenarios [8]. One such input is the Mode-Modulated Input (MoMI), defined as:

$$U (s) = \frac {a (s)}{b (s)}, \tag {11}$$

where $a ( s )$ and $b ( s )$ are coprime polynomials, and $b ( s )$ must have at least one root that is not shared with the poles of $A ( \alpha )$ under any scenario. This design ensures that the resulting system outputs remain distinguishable across different contingency scenarios due to non-overlapping modal responses.
