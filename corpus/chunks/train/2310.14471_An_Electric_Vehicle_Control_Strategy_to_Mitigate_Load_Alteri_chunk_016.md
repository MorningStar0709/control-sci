# B. Case Study 1: Attacks in Absence of Mitigation Scheme

Based on the work presented in [4], [6], and [7], which discuss static attacks, switching attacks, and dynamic attacks respectively, we formulate our attack scenarios against the power grid in the absence of the suggested mitigation strategy to demonstrate their impact as a base case. The detailed mathematical formulation of the attacks is found in [4], [6], and [7]. The first batch of attacks is simulated in the absence of the PSS and the impacts are discussed in the following three cases.

1) Attack 1: is an 800MW static attack initiated at t=5s split on buses 3, 4, 24, and 29. This attack is a single spike in load equal to 13% of the system demand. The attack causes a drop in frequency and the oscillations presented in Fig. 2(a).   
2) Attack 2: is an 800MW switching attack split equally on buses 3, 4, 24, and 29. Fig. 2(b) demonstrates how this attack caused the generators to oscillate against each other and a large frequency deviation that reached 71Hz. This attack would cause the generators to trip resulting in a blackout.   
3) Attack 3: is an 800MW dynamic attack split on buses 3, 4, 24, and 29. In this attack, the loads do not always oscillate in phase. Fig. 2(c) demonstrates the attack impact in which the generators’ frequencies oscillate wildly and continue to grow to reach 74Hz. This trip the generators and cause severe damage.

The second batch of attacks repeats the same three scenarios but in the presence of the PSS. The PSS is meant to stabilize the grid by damping generator frequency swings, but as we demonstrate, it falls short of eliminating LA attack impacts.
