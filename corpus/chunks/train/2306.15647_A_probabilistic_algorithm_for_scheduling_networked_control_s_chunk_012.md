Remark 4: Our design of a scheduling logic is through a probabilistic algorithm. A scheduling logic obtained from Algorithm 1 is neither static nor dynamic (The reader is referred to Section I for a description of these terms). Indeed, we do not repeat a finite length allocation scheme or take properties of the plants or other components in the NCS into consideration at every time instant. Our design is, however, close in spirit to static scheduling techniques in the following sense: (a) computation of the sets, $s _ { j } , ~ j ~ = ~ 1 , 2 , \ldots , \nu$ and the probabilities, $p _ { j } , ~ j ~ = ~ 1 , 2 , \ldots , \nu$ are performed offline prior to execution of the scheduling algorithm, and (b) a logic, 𝛾, obtained from Algorithm 1 does not adapt to unforeseen changes/faults in the plants or other components in the NCS.

In the absence of data losses, design of a scheduling logic, 𝛾, that ensures stochastic stability of each plant in an NCS, follows as a special case of Theorem 1.

Corollary 1: Consider an NCS described in Section II. Let the plant dynamics, $( A _ { i } , B _ { i } ) , i \ = \ 1 , 2 , . . . , N$ , the controller dynamics, $K _ { i } , \ i \ = \ 1 , 2 , \ldots , N .$ , and the capacity of the communication network, 𝑀, be given. Let the probability of data loss, $p = 0$ . Each plant $i = 1 , 2 , \dots , N$ is stochastically stable under a scheduling logic, 𝛾, obtained from Algorithm 1 if and only if for each $i \in s _ { j } , j = 1 , 2 , \ldots , \nu ,$ , there exist symmetric and positive definite matrices, $P _ { k } \in \mathbb { R } ^ { d _ { i } \times d _ { i } }$ , $k = i _ { s } , i _ { u } .$ , such that

$$A _ {k} ^ {\top} \tilde {\mathcal {P}} ^ {i} A _ {k} - P _ {k} \prec 0 _ {d _ {i} \times d _ {i}}, \tag {4}$$

where $\tilde { \mathcal { P } } ^ { i } = p _ { j } P _ { i _ { s } } + ( 1 - p _ { j } ) P _ { i _ { u } }$ .

Proof: Since 𝑞 = 0,

$$
\begin{array}{l} \tilde {\mathcal {P}} ^ {i} = p _ {j} P _ {i _ {s}} + (1 - p _ {j}) P _ {i _ {u}} \\ = p _ {j} (1 - 0) P _ {i _ {s}} + \big ((1 - p _ {j}) + 0 \big) P _ {i _ {u}} \\ = p _ {j} (1 - q) P _ {i _ {s}} + \big ((1 - p _ {j}) + p _ {j} q \big) P _ {i _ {u}}. \\ \end{array}
$$

The assertion of Corollary 1 follows directly from Theorem 1.

Remark 5: Notice that Corollary 1 is more general than [13, Theorem 1]. Indeed, Corollary 1 does not require the capacity of the communication network, 𝑀, to divide the number of plants, $N , ( \mathrm { i . e . , } N ^ { \mathrm { q } } \mathrm { o } M = 0 )$ and hence caters to a larger class of NCSs.
