# Example 5.3 The Diophantine equation

Consider the equation

$$3 x + 2 y = 5 \tag {5.10}$$

where $x$ and $y$ are integers. When does this equation have a solution? A clue is obtained by first considering $x$ and $y$ as real variables. The equation has infinitely many solutions, which can be represented as points on the straight line defined by the equation. By returning to integers it is obvious that $x = 1$ and $y = 1$ is a solution. Another solution may be found by increasing $x$ by 2 and decreasing $y$ by 3. Hence, if $x_0$ and $y_0$ satisfy (5.10), then another solution is given by

$$
\begin{array}{l} x = x _ {0} + 2 n \tag {5.11} \\ y = y _ {0} - 3 n \\ \end{array}
$$

where $n$ is an arbitrary integer. A few solutions follow:

$$
\begin{array}{l} \mathrm{x}: \quad - 5 \quad - 3 \quad - 1 \quad 1 \quad 3 \quad 5 \quad 7 \\ \mathrm{y}: \quad 1 0 \quad 7 \quad 4 \quad 1 \quad - 2 \quad - 5 \quad - 8 \\ \end{array}
$$

We thus find that if the equation has one solution there are infinitely many other solutions. It follows from (5.11) that if a solution $x_{0}, y_{0}$ is known, it is possible to add or subtract 2 from $x_{0}$ until a unique solution with

$$0 \leq x < 2$$

is obtained. Similarly, there is also a unique solution such that

$$0 \leq y < 3$$

Another example shows that there may not be a solution to an equation such as (5.10).
