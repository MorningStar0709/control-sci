A few publications exist regarding control concepts for autonomous racing with combustion engine vehicles. The trajectory-tracking control methods used in this context include model predictive control [18]–[22], linear quadratic regulator [23] and pure pursuit control [24]. These approaches typically output either a longitudinal acceleration [19], [20], [22], [23] or a longitudinal force [18], [21]. Pierini et al. [21] utilize an inverted engine map lookup table and brake characteristics for a feedforward calculation of the low-level control commands. Chung et al. [23] combine such a feedforward structure with a feedback PID controller, while Raji et al. [19] additionally consider driving resistances impacting the vehicle.

However, none of these concepts explicitly account for the engine’s drag torque or varying brake disc friction. Raji et al. [19] limit the longitudinal force when calculating brake pressure and throttle using a pacejka tire model. Nevertheless, this model needs an accurate estimate of the tire friction coefficient, which is usually chosen constant because of the lack of more suitable information. If an unexpected change in the friction occurs, this controller cannot react accordingly.

Consequently, none of the existent control concepts provide a performant and safe longitudinal control method capable of addressing all challenges arising when a realworld racecar operates at its dynamic limits.
