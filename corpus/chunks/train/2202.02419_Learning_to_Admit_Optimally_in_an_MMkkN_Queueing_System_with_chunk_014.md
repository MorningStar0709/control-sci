Based on this observation, we see that when the buffer size N is zero, identifying the best-in-class policy in Π is equivalent to finding the optimal policy. However, when $N > 0$ , the optimal policy that maximizes the long-term average reward is a thresholdbased admission policy, which does not belong to the set Π. The optimal threshold—see Knudsen (1972)—is a complex function of arrival and service rates, and also the cost and reward parameters. Using the optimal threshold to obtain low-regret whilst learning unknown system parameters is complicated, and left for future work. Consequently, in this scenario, we also focus on learning whether it is better to accept all arrivals or reject all. In Section 4.1, we argue that when the service rate is known, the best-in-class policy of the dispatcher is to accept all arrivals if $\mu > \mu ^ { * }$ (subject to availability) and block all arrivals if $\mu < \mu ^ { * }$ , for some positive $\mu ^ { * }$ derived in Section 4.1. We evaluate the performance of a candidate policy with respect to the best-in-class policy, denoted by $\pi ^ { * }$ . In Sections 3.1 and 4.1, we propose a dispatching policy that uses past observations to learn the best-in-class policy, and in Sections 3.2.1 and 4.2.1, we show the asymptotic optimality of our policy by proving its convergence to $\pi ^ { * }$ . Further, in Sections 3.2.2 and 4.2.2, the finite-time performance of our policy is evaluated using the following definition. Our metric focuses on the inaccuracies in the decisions instead of the payoffs, as the latter will likely be continuous around the indifference parameter $\mu ^ { * }$ .

Definition 1. Set $A _ { i } ^ { \pi }$ as the action taken at arrival i in a system that follows policy π. The expected regret of policy π with respect to the best-in-class policy $\pi ^ { * }$ after n arrivals is given by

$$\mathbb {E} \left[ \mathcal {R} (n); \pi \right] = \Big | \mathbb {E} \left[ \sum_ {i = 0} ^ {n - 1} (A _ {i} ^ {\pi} - A _ {i} ^ {\pi^ {*}}) \right] \Big |.$$
