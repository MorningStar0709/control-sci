The methods of conducting stability analysis have evolved in multiple dimensions. One of the recent developments is the incremental stability analysis [6], [7], which is helpful in many applications where it is difficult to find the equilibrium state. To guarantee/analyse incremental behaviour, contraction theory has become an increasingly popular method. Unlike traditional Lyapunov stability analysis, which focuses on a system’s attractor, i.e., an equilibrium point, contraction analysis examines the relative convergence of trajectories of a system. Various techniques exist for analysing the contractivity of smooth dynamical systems, such as those found in [8, 9]. It has also been widely applied to consensus and synchronisation problems of networks [10, 11]. For singularly perturbed nonlinear systems, high-gain observers based on contraction theory are designed in [12]. The incremental stability has also been explored as a design methodology to guarantee certain performance measures [13, 14]. However, contrary to the applications of contraction theory to smooth systems, some work has been done in the switched system setting. For example, logarithmic norm-based contraction analysis for piecewise smooth systems has been formalised in [15], [16] and [17]. The concept of mode-dependent average dwell time for time-dependent switched systems was introduced in [18] based on contraction theory. In [19], the contraction of continuous-time dynamical systems with impulsive control is studied.
