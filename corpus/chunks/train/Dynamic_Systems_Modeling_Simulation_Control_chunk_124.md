The induced or electromagnetic force is the basis of electromechanical actuators such as DC motors and solenoids.

Figure 3.17 illustrates the third basic current–magnetism relationship by showing a wire that is moving relative to magnetic field B. The voltage induced on the moving wire is

$$e _ {b} = (\mathbf {v} \times \mathbf {B}) \cdot \boldsymbol {\ell} \tag {3.81}$$

where v is the velocity vector of the wire. The induced voltage $e _ { b }$ is the “dot” or scalar product of the velocity–magnetic field cross product and vector ??. The cross product v × B establishes the direction of the positive (+) polarity of the induced voltage $e _ { b }$ shown in Fig. 3.17, or the direction of current caused by the induced voltage. We can better understand the induced-voltage effect with the stationary current-carrying wire in Fig. 3.16. Equation (3.79) and Fig. 3.16 show that current vector I?? and magnetic field B induce the force F, which in turn will cause the wire to move with velocity v shown in Fig. 3.17. Equation (3.81) and Fig. 3.17 show that velocity and magnetic field interaction induces a voltage $e _ { b }$ that opposes the current vector I?? in Fig. 3.16 that originally established the induced force F. Hence, the induced voltage $e _ { b }$ is traditionally called the back electromotive force, or back emf. The reader should note that “electromotive force” (emf) is a rather old term that is synonymous with voltage. It should also be noted that motion of the wire can be caused by an external applied force (rather than the induced electromagnetic force as described here), such as steam-powered turbines used in electrical power-generation plants.
