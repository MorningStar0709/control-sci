$$\frac {d ^ {2} X _ {E}}{d t ^ {2}} = (A _ {X} \cos \gamma \cos \tau - A _ {Y} \sin \tau - A _ {Z} \sin \gamma \cos \tau) g, \tag {3.19a}\frac {d ^ {2} Y _ {E}}{d t ^ {2}} = (A _ {X} \cos \gamma \sin \tau + A _ {Y} \cos \tau - A _ {Z} \sin \gamma \sin \tau) g, \tag {3.19b}\frac {d ^ {2} Z _ {E}}{d t ^ {2}} = (1 - A _ {X} \sin \gamma - A _ {Z} \cos \gamma) g, \tag {3.19c}$$

where g is the gravitational acceleration and γ is the flight path angle in the vertical plane. Integration of (3.19a)–(3.19c) yield the velocities $d X _ { E } / d t , d Y _ { E } / d t$ , and $d Z _ { E } / d t$ . The velocities can then be integrated to obtain the missile position coordinates $X _ { E } , Y _ { E }$ , and $Z _ { E }$ . The angle of attack (α) and sideslip $( \beta )$ can be defined in terms of the velocity components as shown in Figure 3.3. Mathematically, the equations for these angles are given in the form

$$\alpha = \tan^ {- 1} (w / u), \tag {3.20a}\beta = \sin^ {- 1} (v / V _ {M}), \tag {3.20b}$$

where $V _ { M } = ( u ^ { 2 } + v ^ { 2 } + w ^ { 2 } ) ^ { 1 / 2 }$ . If the angle of attack and sideslip are small, say, $< 1 5 ^ { \circ }$ , then (3.20a) and (3.20b) assume the simpler form

$$\alpha = w / u, \tag {3.21a}\beta = v / u, \tag {3.21b}$$

where α and $\beta$ are given in radians. Angle of attack and sideslip completely define the attitude of the vehicle with respect to the velocity vector. These angles can also be expressed in terms of the other angles indicated in Figure 3.5 (see also Figure 2.1(b)) as follows:
