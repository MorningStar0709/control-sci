# 3.1. The Hidden Prediction State

We assume that the environment that generates the disturbance w is a dynamic system which can be described by the following equations,

$$z _ {k + 1} = f _ {E} (z _ {k}, r _ {k})o _ {k} = h _ {E o} \left(z _ {k}, r _ {k}\right) \tag {6}w _ {k} = h _ {E w} (z _ {k}, r _ {k}),$$

where $z _ { k }$ is the state of the environment system, $r _ { 0 } , r _ { 1 } , . . . , r _ { N - 1 }$ are independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) random disturbance, $o _ { k } \in \mathbb { O }$ is the measured output, which is also called the observation of the environment, and $w _ { k }$ is the output of the environment system that impacts our system to be controlled. Since we assume that the value of $w _ { k }$ is measured, $O _ { k }$ contains all the information of $w _ { k }$ . From the point of view of the system to be controlled, $w _ { k }$ is the generalized disturbance. Since the environment is usually very complex, such as the human beings and the weather system, the dimensions of z and r can be very high and the exact formulas of (6) may never be known.

Let us define the hidden prediction state

$$
s = \left[ \begin{array}{l l l l l} z _ {0} ^ {T} & r _ {0} ^ {T} & r _ {1} ^ {T} & \dots & r _ {N - 1} ^ {T} \end{array} \right], \tag {7}
$$

as shown in Fig. 3. According to the environment model in (6), the generalized disturbance sequence ¯w can be completely determined by the hidden prediction state, which is a random vector, $s \sim S$ . Therefore, we write $\bar { w } ( s )$ as ¯w is uniquely determined when s is given. The observations o can also be completely determined by s. Therefore, we write

$$o _ {k} (s) = h _ {E o} (z _ {k}, r _ {k}), \tag {8}$$
