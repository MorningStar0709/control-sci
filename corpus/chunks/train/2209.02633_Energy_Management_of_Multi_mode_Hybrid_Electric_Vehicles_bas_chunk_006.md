# 2.1 The energy flow model

In general, the energy flow of the vehicle is modeled based on longitudinal vehicle dynamics, and the force demand, $F _ { d e m } ( \mathrm { t } )$ , the power demand, $P _ { d e m } ( \mathrm { t } )$ , and the torque demand, $T _ { d e m } ( \mathfrak { t } )$ can be calculated by

$$F _ {d e m} (t) = m g f + \frac {1}{2} \rho A _ {f} C _ {d} v ^ {2} (t) + m g \alpha + m a \tag {1}P _ {d e m} (t) = F _ {d e m} (t) \cdot v \tag {2}T _ {d e m} (t) = F _ {d e m} (t) \cdot R \tag {3}$$

where m is the vehicle mass; a is the vehicle acceleration, $g$ is the gravity acceleration; $f$ is the rolling resistance coefficient; $\rho$ is the air density; $A _ { f }$ is the front area of the vehicle; $C _ { d }$ is the air resistance coefficient; ?? is the longitudinal velocity; ?? is the road slope; and ?? is the wheel radius.

In the series mode, the clutch is disengaged, the MG2 solely provides the powertrain for the vehicle to achieve the power demand, and the engine and MG1 works together to keep the SOC, thus, the energy flow is described as:

$$
\left. \begin{array}{c} P _ {d e m} (t) = P _ {m o t 2} (t) \\ T _ {d e m} (t) = i _ {2} \cdot T _ {m o t 2} (t) \\ P _ {e n g} (t) = \frac {n _ {e n g} (t) \cdot T _ {e n g} (t)}{9 5 5 0} \\ P _ {e n g} (t) = P _ {m o t 1} (t) \\ n _ {e n g} (t) = n _ {m o t 1} (t) \end{array} \right\} \tag {4}
$$

In the parallel mode, the engine and MG2 both drive the powertrain by the clutch engagement to achieve the power demand, thus, the energy flow is described as:

$$
\left. \begin{array}{l} P _ {d e m} (t) = P _ {m o t 2} (t) + \left(P _ {e n g} (t) - P _ {m o t 1} (t)\right) \\ T _ {d e m} (t) = i _ {1} \cdot \left(T _ {e n g} (t) - T _ {m o t 1} (t)\right) + i _ {2} \cdot T _ {m o t 2} (t) \\ P _ {e n g} (t) = \frac {n _ {e n g} (t) \cdot T _ {e n g} (t)}{9 5 5 0} \\ n _ {e n g} (t) = n _ {m o t 1} (t) \end{array} \right\} \tag {5}
$$

where, $P _ { m o t 1 , 2 } ( t )$ and $P _ { e n g } ( t )$ are the electric power of MG1 and MG2, respectively; $n _ { m o t 1 } ( t ) \ , \ n _ { m o t 2 } ( t )$ , and $n _ { e n g } ( t )$ are the rotational speed of MG1, MG2, and the engine, respectively; the MG1 and MG2 torque are separately $T _ { m o t 1 , 2 } ( t )$ and $T _ { e n g } ( \mathrm { t } )$ is the engine torque; $i _ { 1 }$ is the transmission ratio of the gearbox to MG1, $i _ { 2 }$ is the final ratio of the gearbox from MG2 to the wheels.

The energy of multi-mode PHEV is from the battery and the engine (fuel tank), and the total power loss mainly consists of the engine loss, $L o s s _ { e n g } ( t )$ , and battery loss, $L o s s _ { b a t t } ( t )$ , which can be calculated by

$$
\left. \begin{array}{c} P _ {l o s s} (t) = L o s s _ {e n g} (t) + L o s s _ {b a t t} (t) \\ L o s s _ {e n g} (t) = m _ {f} (t) \cdot H _ {f} - \frac {n _ {e n g} (t) \cdot T _ {e n g} (t)}{9 5 5 0} \\ L o s s _ {b a t t} (t) = R \cdot I _ {b a t t} (t) ^ {2} \end{array} \right\} \tag {6}
$$

where, $P _ { l o s s }$ is the total power loss; $H _ { f }$ is the heat value of fuel $( H _ { f } = 4 3 . 5 \times 1 0 ^ { 6 } \mathsf { J } / \mathsf { k g } )$ ; and ?? is the equivalent internal resistance in the battery model.
