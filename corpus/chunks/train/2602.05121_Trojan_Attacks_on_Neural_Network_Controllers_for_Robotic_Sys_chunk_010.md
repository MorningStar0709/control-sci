# III. PROPOSED BACKDOOR TROJAN ATTACK DESIGN

The attack is realized through a backdoor trigger embedded within the Trojan network. The trigger mechanism is characterized by the following properties:

• Input Space: The trigger condition is defined in the space of the controller’s inputs, specifically the robot’s current pose $( x _ { r } , y _ { r } , \theta _ { r } )$ and the desired goal $( x _ { d } , y _ { d } )$ .   
• Specificity: Triggers are designed to be highly specific, restricting activation to a narrowly defined subset of the controller input space. Such specificity reduces the likelihood of accidental activation during normal operation. Example trigger conditions may include the robot entering a small spatial region, approaching a particular waypoint with a prescribed orientation, or meeting a conjunction of pose and goal parameters that would be improbable under normal navigation behavior.   
• Stealth: The trigger condition is crafted to appear as a rare but plausible system state, making it difficult to exhaustively test for during quality assurance.

The precision of this trigger design ensures that the Trojan network produces no anomalous effects during regular navigation, testing, or calibration. As a result, the attack can remain dormant for long periods, activating only once when the robot reaches the adversary’s target configuration. For a single destructive event, such as causing one deliberate collision, the system must behave perfectly normal beforehand, making reliability and stealth critical properties of the trigger mechanism. Upon detecting the trigger condition, the Trojan network activates and modifies the control signal generated by the primary controller, applying a multiplicative factor to the wheel velocities to achieve the adversarial objective (e.g., immobilization or hazardous acceleration).
