DeePC and its general DDPC variants have demonstrated promising experimental results for controlling systems beyond LTI settings in various real-world applications, e.g., robotics [27, 28], mixed traffic systems [29, 30], power systems [31, 32]. The recent work [33] has established some theoretical guarantees for DDPC in nonlinear systems. For non-deterministic or nonlinear systems, more offline and online collected data are needed to increase the width (i.e., column number) and depth (i.e., row number) of the trajectory library, so that an accurate enough data-driven representation can be constructed. The benefits of increasing its width are wellrecognized in the literature [29, 31, 34, 35], and the recent works have further emphasized the importance of enlarging its depth [14, 36].

Since the original DeePC in [17], numerous regularization strategies for DDPC have been developed [1, 32, 35, 37– 40]. Early approaches based on $l _ { 1 } -$ and l2-norm penalties were primarily heuristic, which aim to improve empirical control performance and ensure numerical stability for systems beyond LTI settings [17, 32, 35, 38]. A novel projection-based regularizer was later proposed in [1], establishing a formal connection between DeePC and SPC; in fact, the two formulations are equivalent when the weighting parameter is sufficiently large. Another line of work introduced γ-DDPC [37], which reformulates the original data-driven representation via an LQ factorization and introduces a new decision variable γ. Regularization is then applied directly to $\gamma ,$ which offers a potentially transparent interpretation of the regularization effects. Moreover, it is shown in [39, Theorem 2] that $\gamma \cdot$ -DDPC with l2 regularization and a sufficiently large weight is equivalent to SPC. This framework was further extended in [39] by incorporating causality constraints and designing an associated regularizer. More recently, [40] proposed a maximum-likelihood estimator that characterizes future input-output trajectories through an iterative algorithm.
