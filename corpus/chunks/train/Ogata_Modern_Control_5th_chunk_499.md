Nonminimum-Phase Transfer Functions. If, at the high-frequency end, the computed phase lag is $1 8 0 ^ { \circ }$ less than the experimentally obtained phase lag, then one of the zeros of the transfer function should have been in the right-half s plane instead of the left-half s plane.

If the computed phase lag differed from the experimentally obtained phase lag by a constant rate of change of phase, then transport lag, or dead time, is present. If we assume the transfer function to be of the form

$$G (s) e ^ {- T s}$$

where $G ( s )$ is a ratio of two polynomials in s, then

$$
\begin{array}{l} \lim _ {\omega \rightarrow \infty} \frac {d}{d \omega} \underline {{{\left\lfloor G (j \omega) e ^ {- j \omega T} \right.}}} = \lim _ {\omega \rightarrow \infty} \frac {d}{d \omega} \left[ \underline {{{\left\lfloor G (j \omega) \right.}}} + \underline {{{\left\lfloor e ^ {- j \omega T} \right.}}} \right] \\ = \lim _ {\omega \rightarrow \infty} \frac {d}{d \omega} \left[ \underline {{\angle G (j \omega)}} - \omega T \right] \\ = 0 - T = - T \\ \end{array}
$$

where we used the fact that $\operatorname* { l i m } _ { \omega \to \infty } / G ( j \omega ) =$ constant. Thus, from this last equation, we can evaluate the magnitude of the transport lag T.
