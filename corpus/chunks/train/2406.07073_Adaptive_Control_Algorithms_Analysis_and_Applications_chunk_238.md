# 5.3 Identification Methods (Type I)

$$
\begin{array}{l} \hat {y} ^ {0} (t + 1) = - \hat {A} ^ {*} (q ^ {- 1}, t) \hat {y} (t) + \hat {B} ^ {*} (q ^ {- 1}, t) u (t - d) \\ + [ \hat {C} ^ {*} (q ^ {- 1}, t) \varepsilon (t) - \hat {A} ^ {*} (q ^ {- 1}, t) [ y (t) - \hat {y} (t) ] ] \tag {5.32} \\ \end{array}
$$

Defining:

$$\hat {H} ^ {*} (q ^ {- 1}) = \hat {C} ^ {*} (q ^ {- 1}, t) - \hat {A} ^ {*} (q ^ {- 1}, t) = \hat {h} _ {1} (t) + q ^ {- 1} \hat {h} _ {2} (t) + \dots$$

with:

$$\hat {h} _ {i} (t) = \hat {c} _ {i} (t) - \hat {a} _ {i} (t); \quad i = 1, 2, \ldots , \max (n _ {A}, n _ {C})$$

Equation (5.32) can be rewritten as:

$$
\begin{array}{l} \hat {y} ^ {0} (t + 1) = - \hat {A} ^ {*} (q ^ {- 1}, t) \hat {y} (t) + \hat {B} ^ {*} (q ^ {- 1}, t) u (t - d) + \hat {H} ^ {*} (q ^ {- 1}) \varepsilon (t) \\ = \hat {\theta} ^ {T} (t) \phi (t) \tag {5.33} \\ \end{array}
$$

where now:

$$\hat {\theta} ^ {T} (t) = [ \hat {a} _ {1} (t), \dots , \hat {a} _ {n _ {A}}, \hat {b} _ {1} (t), \dots , \hat {b} _ {n _ {B}} (t), \hat {h} _ {1} (t), \dots , \hat {h} _ {n _ {H}} (t) ]\phi^ {T} (t) = [ - \hat {y} (t), \dots , \hat {y} (t - n _ {A} + 1), u (t - d), \dots , u (t - d - n _ {B} + 1),\varepsilon (t), \ldots , \varepsilon (t - n _ {C} + 1) ]$$

Equation (5.33) corresponds to the adjustable predictor for the output error with extended prediction model.

Despite their similar asymptotic properties, there is a difference in the first $n _ { A }$ components of the vector $\phi ( t )$ . While ELS uses the measurements $y ( t ) , y ( t - 1 )$ , . . . directly affected by the disturbance, the OEEPM uses the predicted a posteriori outputs $\hat { y } ( t ) , \hat { y } ( t - 1 )$ which depend upon the disturbance only indirectly through the P.A.A. This explains why a better estimation is obtained with OEEPM than with ELS over short or medium time horizons (it removes the bias more quickly).
