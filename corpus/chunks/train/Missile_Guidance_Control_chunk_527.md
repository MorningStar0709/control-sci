where as was shown by (6.37), n is the mean motion; that is,

$$n = 2 \pi / P = \sqrt {\mu / a ^ {3}},$$

where n is given in radians per unit time and P is the period of orbit (or period of revolution in the ellipse). If P is expressed in mean solar days, then n is called the mean daily motion.

From (6.43) we can also write

$$
\begin{array}{l} n t _ {f f} = (E _ {2} - E _ {1}) - e (\sin E _ {2} - \sin E _ {1}) \\ = (\varepsilon - \delta) - 2 \sin \left(\frac {\varepsilon - \delta}{2}\right) \cos \left(\frac {\varepsilon + \delta}{2}\right), \\ \end{array}
$$

or

$$n t _ {f f} = (\varepsilon - \delta) - (\sin \varepsilon - \sin \delta). \tag {6.51}$$

The angles ε and δ are given by (6.49a) and (6.49b) in terms of $( r _ { 1 } + r _ { 2 } )$ , c, and a. Equations (6.50) and (6.51) constitute Lambert’s theorem for elliptic motion [10], [17].
