$$S _ {i} = \frac {\operatorname{Var} _ {i}}{\operatorname{Var}} = \frac {\operatorname{Var} _ {X _ {i}} \left(E _ {\boldsymbol {X} _ {\sim i}} (Y \mid X _ {i})\right)}{\operatorname{Var}} \tag {31a}
\begin{array}{l} S _ {T i} = \frac {\operatorname{Var} _ {i}}{\operatorname{Var}} + \sum_ {1 \leq i <   j \leq k} ^ {k} \frac {\operatorname{Var} _ {i , j}}{\operatorname{Var}} + \dots + \frac {\operatorname{Var} _ {1 , 2 , \cdots , k}}{\operatorname{Var}} \\ = \frac {E _ {\boldsymbol {X} _ {\sim i}} \left(\operatorname{Var} _ {X _ {i}} \left(Y \mid \boldsymbol {X} _ {\sim i}\right)\right)}{\operatorname{Var}} \tag {31b} \\ \end{array}
$$

where $S _ { i }$ reflects the effect of varying $X _ { i }$ alone on the output variance, $S _ { T i }$ measures the contribution of $X _ { i }$ plus all higher-order effects with $X _ { i }$ to the output variance, $V a r _ { X _ { i } } \left( E _ { X _ { \sim i } } \left( Y | X _ { i } \right) \right)$ represents the expected reduction in variance if ${ \dot { X } } _ { i }$ could be fixed, and $E _ { X _ { \sim i } } \left( V a r _ { X _ { i } } \left( Y | X _ { \sim i } \right) \right)$ represents the expected value of the left variance if all variables but $X _ { i }$ could be fixed [43].

In practice, the Monte Carlo estimator shown in (32) is widely used to simultaneously compute indices in (31), which is derived in [44].

$$\operatorname{Var} _ {X _ {i}} \left(E _ {\boldsymbol {X} _ {\sim i}}\right) \approx \frac {1}{M} \sum_ {j = 1} ^ {M} g (\boldsymbol {B}) _ {j} \left(g \left(\boldsymbol {A} _ {\boldsymbol {B}} ^ {i}\right) _ {j} - g (\boldsymbol {A}) _ {j}\right) \tag {32a}E _ {\boldsymbol {X} \sim i} \left(\operatorname{Var} _ {X _ {i}}\right) \approx \frac {1}{2 M} \sum_ {j = 1} ^ {M} \left(g (\boldsymbol {A}) _ {j} - g \left(\boldsymbol {A} _ {\boldsymbol {B}} ^ {i}\right) _ {j}\right) ^ {2} \tag {32b}$$

where M is the number of sampling points, A and B are sampling matrices.
