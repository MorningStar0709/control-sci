The reactive power control strategies will switch when the wind turbine enters and exits LVRT [15]. This switching control system contains continuous dynamic processes and discrete switching events [16]. Thus, switched system theory can be introduced to characterize the dynamic voltage evolution and analyze the stability of grid-connected wind power systems. Hybrid systems are dynamic systems with hierarchical structures whose states and outputs depend on continuous dynamics, discrete events, and their interactions [17]–[19]. The switched system, a significant category of hybrid systems, consists of a family of continuous-time subsystems and discrete switching laws indicating the active subsystem at each instant of time [20], [21]. According to different switching modes, switched systems can be classified as state-dependent or timedependent [21], [22]. The modeling procedures and system state trajectory-based dynamic evolution analysis methods for switched systems are introduced in [23]–[25]. The last decades have seen extensive research on stability analysis of switched systems, which can be roughly divided into two main problems: one is to find the stability criteria for switched systems under given or arbitrary switching laws; the other is to identify switching laws making switched systems asymptotically stable [22], [26], [27]. Powerful analysis tools such as common Lyapunov functions and multiple Lyapunov functions have been developed to address these problems [22], [28], [29]. Other active research fields, including observability [30], controllability [31], reachability [32], feedback stabilization [33], and optimal control [34] for switched systems, are also in the ascendant.

In summary, existing studies have pointed out that the switching of power control strategies is the root cause of voltage oscillations induced by repeated LVRT. However, there is still a lack of quantitative analysis relating this phenomenon to the dynamic control parameters of the wind turbine. Therefore, this paper introduces switched system theory for model construction, mechanism elucidation, and stability analysis of the LVRT switching process, providing a dynamic and quantitative perspective on these issues.
