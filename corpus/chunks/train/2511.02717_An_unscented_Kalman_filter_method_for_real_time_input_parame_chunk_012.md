Figure 1: Linear 3-DOF sytem excited by a pulse at DOF 3.

For the numerical application of the proposed methodology consider the 3-DOF system of Fig. 1 which is described by the following equation:

$$
\mathbf {M} \left\{ \begin{array}{l} \ddot {x _ {1}} (t) \\ \ddot {x _ {2}} (t) \\ \ddot {x _ {3}} (t) \end{array} \right\} + \mathbf {C} \left\{ \begin{array}{l} \dot {x _ {1}} (t) \\ \dot {x _ {2}} (t) \\ \dot {x _ {3}} (t) \end{array} \right\} + \mathbf {K} \left\{ \begin{array}{l} x _ {1} (t) \\ x _ {2} (t) \\ x _ {3} (t) \end{array} \right\} = \left\{ \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 0 \\ u _ {3} (t) \end{array} \right\} \tag {13}
$$

where the system matrices which need to be identied (apart from M) are:

$$
\mathbf {M} = \left[ \begin{array}{c c c} m _ {1} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & m _ {2} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & m _ {3} \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array}{c c c} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array} \right], \quad \mathbf {C} = \left[ \begin{array}{c c c} c _ {1} + c _ {2} & - c _ {2} & 0 \\ - c _ {2} & c _ {2} + c _ {3} & - c _ {3} \\ 0 & - c _ {3} & c _ {3} \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array}{c c c} 0. 2 5 + 0. 5 & - 0. 5 & 0 \\ - 0. 5 & 0. 5 + 0. 7 5 & - 0. 7 5 \\ 0 & - 0. 7 5 & 0. 7 5 \end{array} \right],

\mathbf {K} = \left[ \begin{array}{c c c} k _ {1} + k _ {2} & - k _ {2} & 0 \\ - k _ {2} & k _ {2} + k _ {3} & - k _ {3} \\ 0 & - k _ {3} & k _ {3} \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array}{c c c} 9 + 1 1 & - 1 1 & (1 4) \\ - 1 1 & 1 1 + 1 3 & - 1 3 \\ 0 & - 1 3 & 1 3 \end{array} \right]
$$

with initial conditions $\mathbf { x } ( 0 ) = [ 0 \quad 0 \quad 0 ] ^ { T }$ and $\dot { \bf x } ( 0 ) = [ 0 0 0 ] ^ { T }$ . A pulse of 100N for 0.01s is applied at the time instant of $5 s$ , which is not known beforehand.

In order to create synthetic measurements, the Runge Kutta $4 ^ { t h }$ order method of integration is utilized to compute the system response for 30s. The sampling frequency for the dynamic state measurements is considered to be 100 Hz, therefore the time discretization $\Delta t$ used in the Runge Kutta numerical solution is 0.01s. Finally, to consider measurement noise, each response signal is contaminated by a Gaussian white noise sequence with a 5% root-mean-square noise-to-signal ratio.
