# IV. CASE STUDY

As remarked in Section II, finding an adaptive control Lyapunov function – in particular satisfying Assumption 1 – is potentially difficult. Subsequently the approach is more suitable for lower dimensional systems. However, dynamics of special form allow the recursive construction of (adaptive) control Lyapunov functions efficiently as, e. g., in the case of systems in (parametric) strict-feedback form [22, Thm. 3.5]

$$
\begin{array}{l} \dot {x} _ {1} = x _ {2} + F _ {1} ^ {\top} (x _ {1}) \theta \\ \dot {x} _ {2} = x _ {3} + F _ {2} ^ {\top} (x _ {1}, x _ {2}) \theta \\ \begin{array}{c} \bullet \\ \vdots \\ \bullet \end{array} \\ \dot {x} _ {n - 1} = x _ {n} + F _ {n - 1} ^ {\top} (x _ {1}, \dots , x _ {n - 1}) \theta \\ \dot {x} _ {n} = F _ {n} ^ {\top} (x) \theta + g (x) u, \qquad g (x) \neq 0, \forall x. \\ \end{array}
$$

Since the state of the presented practically stabilizing reinforcement learning controller must not, in general, converge to the origin, the infinite-horizon cost (2) may not be finite. Subsequently, another measure of the controller’s performance should be considered. In the comparison of the learning controller to the adaptive control Lyapunov function based controller, only the transient performance until the target set is reached after some time $K \in  { \mathbb { N } } _ { 0 }$ is considered, e. g., by

$$\mathcal {C} _ {\%} (x _ {0}) = \frac {\sum_ {k = 0} ^ {K _ {u}} r (x _ {k} , u _ {k})}{\sum_ {k = 0} ^ {K _ {\mu}} r (x _ {k} , \mu (x _ {k} , \hat {\theta} _ {k}))} \mathrm{d} t, \tag {28}$$

where $K _ { u , \mu } \in \mathbb { N } _ { 0 }$ denote the respective times required until the state reaches $B _ { r }$ from $x _ { 0 } \in B _ { R }$ .
