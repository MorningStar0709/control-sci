where we defined a new variable $H = F \Sigma ^ { - 1 } F ^ { \top }$ which is equivalent to the LMI (30d). Also, the matrix inside the third trace term can be simplified as

$$
\begin{array}{l} K V K ^ {\top} = U _ {0} F \Sigma^ {- 1} V \Sigma^ {- 1} F ^ {\top} U _ {0} ^ {\top} \\ = U _ {0} E U _ {0} ^ {\top}, \tag {33} \\ \end{array}
$$

where we used $E = F S ^ { - 1 } F ^ { \top }$ and $S ^ { - 1 } = \Sigma ^ { - 1 } V \Sigma ^ { - 1 }$ which are equivalents to (30c) and (30e) respectively. As a result of the optimization problem (30), the gain $K = U _ { 0 } F \Sigma ^ { - 1 }$ stabilizes the uncertain data-driven parameterized system (27). Also, according to the analysis in Theorems 1 and 2, this gain provides MSS for the actual closed-loop system (3). □

Theorem 3 provides a principled approach to solving the LQR problem directly from noisy input-output data, without requiring explicit knowledge of the system matrices A and B. By formulating the control objective as an SDP, the result guarantees not only optimality with respect to the quadratic cost but also the MSS of the closed-loop system. The controller gain $K = U _ { 0 } F \Sigma ^ { - 1 }$ is constructed purely from data, and the formulation inherently incorporates robustness against both process and measurement noise through the covariance terms.
