The attractive feature of Theorem 3.1 is that the constrained minimization problem can be handled efficiently by convex optimization solvers such as CVX, [11]–[13]. The main issue of (7) is that the number of unknowns scales as $o ( m ^ { 2 } )$ . This may cause long computation times and numerical precision issues, leading to convergence issues when the order of the FIR part is high. The approach proposed below, overcomes these limitations.

Define the Toeplitz matrix of order $n \geq m$ associated with the coefficients $g = \{ g _ { k } \} _ { k = 0 , \ldots , m - 1 }$ as follows, [21]:

$$
\phi_ {n} (g) = \left[ \begin{array}{c c c c c} g _ {0} & 0 & \dots & \dots & 0 \\ g _ {1} & g _ {0} & 0 & \dots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots \\ g _ {m - 1} & g _ {m - 2} & \dots & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & g _ {m - 1} & \dots & g _ {0} & 0 \\ 0 & \dots & g _ {m - 1} & \dots & g _ {0} \end{array} \right] \in \mathbb {R} ^ {n \times n}. \tag {8}
$$

The first n samples of the response of the FIR filter given by g can be computed as the product between $\phi _ { n } ( g )$ and the (vectorized) input $w _ { n } \ = \ \left[ w ( 0 ) \quad w ( 1 ) \quad \ldots \quad w ( n - 1 ) \right] ^ { T }$ . It is thus not surprising that the passivity of the FIR filter is related to the positive semi-definiteness of the Toeplitz matrix $\phi _ { n } ( g ) + \phi _ { n } ( g ) ^ { T }$ , as stated in the following theorem. In what follows, the symbol σ denotes the minimal singular value of a matrix.
