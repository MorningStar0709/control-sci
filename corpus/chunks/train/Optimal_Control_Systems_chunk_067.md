# 2.1.3 Differential and Variation

Here, we consider the differential of a function and the variation of a functional.

(a) Differential of a Function: Let us define at a point $t^*$ the increment of the function $f$ as

$$\Delta f \triangleq f (t ^ {*} + \Delta t) - f (t ^ {*}). \tag {2.1.10}$$

By expanding $f(t^{*} + \Delta t)$ in a Taylor series about $t^*$ , we get

$$\Delta f = f \left(t ^ {*}\right) + \left(\frac {d f}{d t}\right) _ {*} \Delta t + \frac {1}{2 !} \left(\frac {d ^ {2} f}{d t ^ {2}}\right) _ {*} (\Delta t) ^ {2} + \dots - f \left(t ^ {*}\right). \tag {2.1.11}$$

Neglecting the higher order terms in $\Delta t$ ,

$$\Delta f = \left(\frac {d f}{d t}\right) _ {*} \Delta t = \dot {f} (t ^ {*}) \Delta t = d f. \tag {2.1.12}$$

Here, df is called the differential of f at the point $t^{*}$ . $\dot{f}(t^{*})$ is the derivative or slope of f at $t^{*}$ . In other words, the differential df is the first order approximation to increment $\Delta t$ . Figure 2.1 shows the relation between increment, differential and derivative.
