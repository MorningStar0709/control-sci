# 5.1. MSE: A Poor Measure

if we choose the expected MSE as the one-time prediction performance measure, then,

$$
\begin{array}{l} E _ {M} (\bar {W} _ {t o}, \bar {W} _ {b}) = \hat {\mathbb {E}} _ {\bar {w} \sim \bar {W} _ {t o}} M (\bar {w}, \bar {W} _ {b}) \\ = \underset {\bar {w} \sim \bar {W} _ {t o}} {\hat {\mathbb {E}}} \left[ \underset {\bar {w} _ {b} \sim \bar {W} _ {b}} {\hat {\mathbb {E}}} \| \bar {w} - \bar {w} _ {b} \| _ {2} ^ {2} \right]. \tag {31} \\ \end{array}
$$

In this case, $\hat { W } _ { b } = \hat { W } _ { t o }$ may not even locally minimize $E _ { M } ( \bar { W } _ { t o } , \cdot )$ . This means that when using MSE, even with a large amount of data, we will miss the preditor’s ultimate goal $\hat { W } _ { t o }$ . When there is no constraint, the best $\bar { W } _ { b }$ may be a deterministic prediction, which is inferior to the best stochastic predictor in terms of the control performance. With the proposed framework, examples can be easily constructed to show that the MSE-based measure is neither a best-P-lowest-C measure nor a better-P-lower-C measure (see Section 6).
