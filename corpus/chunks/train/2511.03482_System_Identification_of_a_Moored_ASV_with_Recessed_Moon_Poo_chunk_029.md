The so-obtained PDFs of each variable from the different sources are then compared using the Jensen-Shannon divergence (JSD) [53], defined as:

$$\mathrm{JSD} _ {\xi} = \mathrm{JSD} [ V _ {\xi} | | W _ {\xi} ] = \frac {1}{2} D (V _ {\xi} | | M _ {\xi}) + \frac {1}{2} D (W _ {\xi} | | M _ {\xi}), \tag {29}$$

where

$$M _ {\xi} = \frac {1}{2} (V _ {\xi} + W _ {\xi}), \quad \text { and } \quad D (V _ {\xi} | | W _ {\xi}) = \sum_ {y \in \chi} V _ {\xi} (y) \ln \left(\frac {V _ {\xi} (y)}{W _ {\xi} (y)}\right). \tag {30}$$

The Jensen–Shannon divergence (JSD) [54] is a symmetric measure of similarity between two probability distributions $V$ and W, based on the Kullback–Leibler divergence (D) [55]. Here, $V _ { \xi }$ and $W _ { \xi }$ are the PDFs of the variable $\xi$ from the EFD and HDMDc, respectively. The JSD quantifies the average discrepancy of each distribution with respect to their mixture M, defined over the domain $\chi .$ . It is always finite, bounded as $0 \leq \mathrm { J S D } [ V | | M ] \leq \ln 2$ .
