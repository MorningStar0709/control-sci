Another factor influencing maximum turn rate is the roll orientation of the missile with respect to the maneuver plane. If the direction of the turn is perpendicular to either the pitch or yaw plane, then the turn will be confined only to that plane, and the maximum acceleration will be limited by the autopilot to 25 $g ^ { \mathbf { \prime } } \mathbf { s } .$ . If the direction of the turn is halfway between the two planes, both autopilots will contribute, and the allowable turning acceleration is as high as $2 5 \sqrt { 2 }$ , i.e., about 35 $g ^ { \mathbf { \prime } } \mathbf { s } .$ . Note that the time for which the missile locks on the target can vary from about 0.6 to 1.0 second. Increased lock time can have a significant affect because of the rapidly changing geometry, and usually results in increased missile flight times to attain a successful intercept. Because lock time is an uncontrollable factor, a degree of uncertainty is introduced to the minimum-range zone.

At this point, simple interception model dynamics will be developed. Assuming that the target and the missile motions evolve in the same horizontal plane, the geometry of the interception process is shown in Figure 4.10(b). The interception is characterized by two variables, namely, the target range and the LOS angle. The kinematic equations are expressed by the following relations:

$$
\begin{array}{l} \frac {d r}{d t} = V _ {t} \cos (\lambda - \gamma_ {t}) - u \cos (\lambda - \theta) - w \sin (\lambda - \theta), \\ \frac {d \lambda}{d t} = - \left[ V _ {t} \sin (\lambda - \gamma_ {t}) + u \sin (\lambda - \theta) - w \cos (\lambda - \theta) \right] / r, \\ \end{array}
$$

where

$$r = \text { missile - target range },\lambda = L O S \text { angle },\theta = \text { pitch angle (or missile axis angle) },u = \text { longitudinal velocity component of the missile },w = \text { normal velocity component of the missile }.$$
