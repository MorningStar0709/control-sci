# 2.3 Optimal control for semilinear problems

The proposed OSNN applies also to more complex problems and is fairly flexible with the governing PDEs. We illustrate this with semilinear elliptic problems, one popular model class in PDE optimal control. Consider the following optimal control problem

$$\min J (y, u) := \frac {1}{2} \| y - y _ {d} \| _ {L ^ {2} (\Omega)} ^ {2} + \frac {\lambda}{2} \| u \| _ {L ^ {2} (\Omega)} ^ {2}, \tag {2.10}$$

subject to the following semilinear elliptic PDE constraint

$$- \Delta y = f (y) + u \text { in } \Omega \quad \text { with } \quad y = 0 \text { on } \partial \Omega . \tag {2.11}$$

The function $f : \mathbb { R } \to \mathbb { R }$ should satisfy suitable conditions (e.g., monotone) so that (2.11) is well-posed [49, Theorem 4.4]. Then the optimal control problem has a locally unique solution (¯y, u¯) [49, p. 208]. The KKT system for problem (2.10)–(2.11) is given by [49, p. 216]:

$$
\left\{ \begin{array}{l l} - \Delta y = f (y) + u \text {in} \Omega & \text {with} \quad y = 0 \text {on} \partial \Omega , \\ - \Delta p = f ^ {\prime} (y) p + y - y _ {d} \text {in} \Omega & \text {with} \quad p = 0 \text {on} \partial \Omega , \\ u = - \lambda^ {- 1} p \text {in} \Omega . \end{array} \right.
$$

Like before, the empirical loss $\widehat { L } ( y _ { \theta } , p _ { \sigma } )$ for problem (2.10)–(2.11) is given by

$$
\begin{array}{l} \widehat {\mathcal {L}} (y _ {\theta}, p _ {\sigma}) = \frac {| \Omega |}{n _ {d}} \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {n _ {d}} \left(\Delta y _ {\theta} (X _ {i}) + f (y _ {\theta} (X _ {i})) - \lambda^ {- 1} p _ {\sigma} (X _ {i})\right) ^ {2} + \alpha_ {b} ^ {y} \frac {| \partial \Omega |}{n _ {b}} \sum_ {j = 1} ^ {n _ {b}} \left(y _ {\theta} (Y _ {j})\right) ^ {2} \\ + \alpha_ {i} \frac {| \Omega |}{n _ {d}} \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {n _ {d}} \big (\Delta p _ {\sigma} (X _ {i}) + f ^ {\prime} (y _ {\theta} (X _ {i})) p _ {\sigma} (X _ {i}) + y _ {\theta} (X _ {i}) - y _ {d} (X _ {i}) \big) ^ {2} + \alpha_ {b} ^ {p} \frac {| \partial \Omega |}{n _ {b}} \sum_ {j = 1} ^ {n _ {b}} \big (p _ {\sigma} (Y _ {j}) \big) ^ {2}. \\ \end{array}
$$

The adaptation of the approach to semilinear problems with box constraint on the control U is direct.
