Comparisons to existing work. Compared to the existing work on learning the Kalman filter for output estimation [12–15] based on least squares methods, our approach has the following advantages: 1) our algorithm design based on OGD method is conceptually simpler, and our regret analysis is also more constructive, achieving comparable regret bounds $( \mathrm { e . g . } , \mathcal { O } ( \log ^ { 4 } T )$ in [13] and $\mathcal { O } ( \log ^ { 3 } T )$ in [15]); 2) we do not require a warm-up period in our algorithm (i.e., the regret holds for any $T > 1 )$ or the regularity assumption that the matrix $A - L C$ in the steady-state Kalman filter (see (11)) is diagonalizable [13–15]; 3) our regret holds against the more general finite-step Kalman filter benchmark (see (20)) as apposed to the steady-state Kalman filter benchmark considered in [12–15]. While the regret bounds in [12–15] also hold for open-loop marginally stable system, we argue later in Section 6 that our regret analysis can be extended to handle open-loop unstable system that can be stabilized with closed-loop input. Finally, we stress that the main merit of our algorithmic framework is its ability to handle the state estimation problem that will be studied in Section 5.
