# Attack Scenario and thread model

We consider a scenario in which the robots regularly visit a charging station to both charge and re-localize and correct accumulated localization errors caused by the used odometry. This makes the charging region a critical component of the robot’s navigation pipeline, encompassing the adversary’s objectives and capabilities, as well as the attack surface within a learning-based robotic control system. We assume that an adversary has the following capabilities:

• Supply Chain Compromise: The adversary can infiltrate the model development or deployment pipeline, tampering with the model after training or compromising the model update/distribution mechanism during manufacturing or remote software updates, e.g., see [15], [16].   
• Model Injection: The adversary can inject a malicious neural network component (the Trojan network) into the robot’s control software stack. This component operates in parallel with the primary controller.   
• No Physical Access During Attack: The adversary does not require physical access to the robot during the attack phase, nor the ability to interact with its sensors or actuators in real-time.

The adversary aims to compromise the robot’s safe operation, causing physical damage, operational disruption, or reputational harm to the company deploying the robots. Specifically, the attacker’s objectives can be summarized as follows:

• (O1) Stealth and Evasion: Ensure the malicious behaviour remains undetectable during normal operation, routine testing, and validation procedures to avoid early discovery and removal.   
• (O2) Targeted Activation: Trigger malicious behaviour only under specific, attacker-defined conditions to maintain a high degree of control and minimize the risk of accidental exposure.   
• (O3) Physical Disruption or Damage: Cause the robot to malfunction in a targeted manner, leading to operational downtime, physical damage to the robot or its environment, or safety risks to nearby humans.
