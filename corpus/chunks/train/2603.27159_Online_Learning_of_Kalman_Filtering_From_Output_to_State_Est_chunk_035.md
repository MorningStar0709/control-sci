The role of random queries. Algorithm 2 invokes the gradient-based update for the filter parameter $M _ { t }$ in line 9 using the queried $\tilde { x } _ { t }$ , when the value of the random variable $b _ { i }$ sampled in line 3 satisfies the condition in line 6. In our proof of Theorem 5.2 below, we crucially leverage this randomness in the gradient-based update to extend the regret over the steps when $M _ { t }$ is updated to the steps over the whole time horizon $t = 0 , \ldots , T - 1$ , achieving the desired balance between the number of queries of $\tilde { { x } } _ { t }$ and the regret of Algorithm 2. Such a novel random query (or sampling) scheme also sheds light on online learning problems with costly or limited observations $\left( \mathrm { e . g . , } \left[ 5 7 , 5 8 \right] \right)$ .

Prior knowledge required by Algorithm 2. Similarly to our arguments for Algorithm 2, to achieve the regret in Theorem 5.2, Algorithm 2 requires the knowledge of $\alpha _ { 0 } , \psi , \bar { \sigma }$ given by (13) and the horizon length T . Again, we argue in Appendix D that one can use a doubling trick to achieve the same regret as Theorem 5.2 without the knowledge of T .
