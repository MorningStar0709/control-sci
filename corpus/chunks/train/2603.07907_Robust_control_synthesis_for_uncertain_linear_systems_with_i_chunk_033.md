# A. A Second-Order LFT Uncertain System

In this subsection, a second-order uncertain LFT system is used to illustrate the effectiveness of the proposed robust $\mathcal { H } _ { \infty }$ state-feedback controller. Let us consider a second-order uncertain LFT system with input saturation

$$
\dot {x} = \left[ \begin{array}{c c} 0 & 1 \\ - 1 0 & - 8 \end{array} \right] x + \left[ \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 1 \end{array} \right] p + \left[ \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ - 1 \end{array} \right] d + \left[ \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 0. 1 \end{array} \right] S a t (u)

q = \left[ \begin{array}{c c} 2 & - 1 \end{array} \right] x + d + 0. 3 S a t (u)

e = \left[ \begin{array}{c c} - 1 & 1 \end{array} \right] x + p + 0. 5 d + 0. 1 S a t (u)
p = \Delta q \tag {30}$$

where $S a t ( u )$ denotes the input saturation nonlinearity. By introducing the auxiliary dynamics $\dot { u } = - \alpha u + v$ and the dead-zone function $w = u - S a t ( u )$ , the system (30) can be rewritten as

$$
\left[ \begin{array}{c} \dot {x} \\ \dot {u} \\ q \\ e \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array}{c c c c c c c} 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ - 1 0 & - 8 & 0. 1 & 1 & - 0. 1 & - 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & - \alpha & 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \\ - 2 & - 1 & 0. 3 & 0 & - 0. 3 & 1 & 0 \\ - 1 & 1 & 0. 1 & 1 & - 0. 1 & 0. 5 & 0 \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{c} x \\ u \\ p \\ w \\ d \\ v \end{array} \right]
p = \Delta qw = \mathcal {N} (u) = u - S a t (u) \tag {31}$$

where

$$
A = \left[ \begin{array}{c c c} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ - 1 0 & - 8 & 0. 1 \\ 0 & 0 & - \alpha \end{array} \right], B _ {0} = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 0. 1 \\ 0 \end{array} \right], B _ {1} = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{array} \right]

B _ {2} = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ - 1 \\ 0 \end{array} \right], C _ {0} = \left[ \begin{array}{c c c} 2 & - 1 & 0. 3 \\ - 1 & 1 & 0. 1 \end{array} \right],
D _ {0 0} = 0. 3, D _ {0 1} = 0, D _ {0 2} = 1,D _ {1 0} = 0. 1, D _ {1 1} = 1, D _ {1 2} = 0. 5. \tag {32}$$

As discussed previously, the Popov IQC (8), the Zames–Falb IQC (10), and the sector-bound IQC (9), together with the scaling factors $\lambda _ { 1 } , \lambda _ { 2 } , \lambda _ { 3 }$ , are used to characterize the deadzone nonlinearity $\mathcal { N } ( u )$ , namely, $\Pi _ { \cal N } = \lambda _ { 1 } \Pi _ { \bar { P } } + \lambda _ { 2 } \Pi _ { \bar { Z } \bar { F } } +$ $\lambda _ { 3 } \Pi _ { \bar { S } }$ .
