The representation in (20) is useful if we want to compute expectations of a nonlinear function over a unit Gaussian distribution. It turns out that the expectation of the function can be simply extracted from the zeroth order coefficient $c _ { 0 }$ of the Fourier–Hermite series and the higher order coefficients are equal to the expectations of the derivatives of the function $g ( x )$ [25]. In this paper, we are particularly interested in the second-order Fourier–Hermite series expansion of $g ( x )$ which can be written as

$$
\begin{array}{l} g (x) \approx \sum_ {k = 0} ^ {2} \sum_ {| \mathcal {I} | = k} \frac {1}{\mathcal {I} !} c _ {\mathcal {I}} H _ {\mathcal {I}} (x) = \mathbb {E} [ g (x) ] + \mathbb {E} [ g (x) H _ {1} (x) ] ^ {\top} H _ {1} (x) \\ + \frac {1}{2} \operatorname{tr} \left\{\mathbb {E} [ g (x) H _ {2} (x) ] H _ {2} (x) \right\}. \tag {21} \\ \end{array}
$$

In (21), the multivariate polynomials $H _ { i } ( x )$ have been expressed as vectors and matrices as follows (cf. [25]):

$$H _ {0} (x) = 1, \quad H _ {1} (x) = x, \quad H _ {2} (x) = x x ^ {\top} - I. \tag {22}$$

We can also generalize the expansion to a more general Gaussian distribution $\textstyle { \mathcal { N } } ( { \boldsymbol { \mu } } , { \boldsymbol { \Sigma } } )$ by rewriting the second order Fourier–Hermite expansion as

$$
\begin{array}{l} g (\Lambda x + \mu) \approx \mathbb {E} [ g (\Lambda x + \mu) ] + \mathbb {E} [ g (\Lambda x + \mu) H _ {1} (x) ] ^ {\top} H _ {1} (x) \\ + \frac {1}{2} \operatorname{tr} \left\{\mathbb {E} [ g (\Lambda x + \mu) H _ {2} (x) ] H _ {2} (x) \right\}. \tag {23} \\ \end{array}
$$

$$
\begin{array}{l} g (y) \approx \mathbb {E} [ g (y) ] + \mathbb {E} [ g (y) H _ {1} (\Lambda^ {- 1} (y - \mu)) ] ^ {\top} H _ {1} (\Lambda^ {- 1} (y - \mu)) \\ + \frac {1}{2} \operatorname{tr} \left\{\mathbb {E} [ g (y) H _ {2} (\Lambda^ {- 1} (y - \mu)) ] H _ {2} (\Lambda^ {- 1} (y - \mu)) \right\}, \tag {24} \\ \end{array}
$$
