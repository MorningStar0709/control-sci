$$
\begin{array}{l} \widehat {K} = \widehat {B} ^ {\dagger} \widehat {B} Z = (\frac {B ^ {\intercal} B}{B _ {\ell_ {d}} ^ {2}}) ^ {- 1} \cdot \frac {B ^ {\intercal}}{B _ {\ell_ {d}}} \cdot (s _ {L} B K) \\ = (B _ {\ell_ {d}} s _ {L}) (B ^ {\intercal} B) ^ {- 1} (B ^ {\intercal} B) K = (B _ {\ell_ {d}} s _ {L}) K, \tag {69} \\ \end{array}
$$

which completes the proof.

Theorem 4 reveals that the feedback matrix K in the single-input case can be inferred up to an ambiguity scalar, which involves two factors brought by estimating L and B independently. Note that calculating the ratio of elements in K and B is the key to this conclusion, which is directly feasible in the single input case.

For situations where $B \in \mathbb { R } ^ { n \times m } \ ( m > 1 )$ , the matrices B and K cannot be estimated uniquely due to bilinearity of the equation $B K = Z$ . To deal with this issue, we leverage the full rank constraint of B and K and proceed to approximate them by the SVD factorization. By decomposing the matrix $Z$ and carefully selecting components that ensure the desired rank properties, we can construct solutions for B and K that meet both the equation and the rank constraints. First, applying the SVD on the target matrix Z, we have

$$Z = \tilde {U} \tilde {\Lambda} \tilde {V} ^ {\intercal}, \tag {70}$$

where $\tilde { U } \in \mathbb R ^ { n \times n }$ and $\tilde { V } \in \mathbb R ^ { n \times n }$ are orthogonal matrices, and $\tilde { \boldsymbol { \Lambda } } \in \mathbb { R } ^ { n \times n }$ is a diagonal matrix composed of the singular values of Z. Since the goal is for B and K to both have rank $m ,$ , we can focus on the top m singular values and their corresponding vectors in $\tilde { U }$ and $\tilde { V } .$ . Let $\tilde { U } _ { m }$ denote the first n × m columns of $\tilde { U } , \tilde { V } _ { m }$ denote the first n × m columns of $\tilde { V } ,$ , and $\tilde { \Lambda } _ { m } \in \mathbb { R } ^ { m \times m }$ be the diagonal matrix composed of the top m singular values of $\tilde { \Lambda } .$ Then, we approximate Z by

$$Z \approx \tilde {U} _ {m} \tilde {\Lambda} _ {m} \tilde {V} _ {m} ^ {\intercal}. \tag {71}$$

Using this truncated SVD, we construct B and K as

$$\widehat {B} = \tilde {U} _ {m} \tilde {\Lambda} _ {m} ^ {1 / 2} \quad \text { and } \quad \widehat {K} = \tilde {\Lambda} _ {m} ^ {1 / 2} \tilde {V} _ {m} ^ {T}, \tag {72}$$
