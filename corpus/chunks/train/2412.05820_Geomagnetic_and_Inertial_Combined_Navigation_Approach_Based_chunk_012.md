# 2.1. Geomagnetic Field Model

Constructing an accurate geomagnetic model becomes the basis for achieving geomagnetic navigation. The spherical harmonic analysis method is currently the most commonly used method for constructing global geomagnetic field models (Kaji et al., 2019). The international geomagnetic reference field (IGRF) model and the WMM model (Guo et al., 2013; Zhang et al., 2021a) commonly used by researchers are both based on the spherical harmonic analysis method. The geomagnetic field is characterized by an axial dipole field aligned with the geocentric axis, as shown in Fig. 2.

The geomagnetic navigation system can determine the location through the uniqueness of geomagnetic information. During navigation, the location of the observation point can be determined by the description of geomagnetic information as shown in Eq. (1):

$$\boldsymbol {B} = \left\{B _ {x}, B _ {y}, B _ {z}, B _ {H}, B _ {F}, D, I \right\}. \tag {1}$$

The geomagnetic information vector B is composed of seven elements, specifically: $B _ { x } , B _ { y } , B _ { z }$ represents the geomagnetic field components toward the north, east, and radially inward, respectively; $B _ { H }$ represents the horizontal intensity; $B _ { F }$ represents the total geomagnetic field intensity; D represents the magnetic declination; I represents the magnetic inclination. The magnetic declination D represents the angle formed between the horizontal intensity $B _ { H }$ and the geographic north direction x. The magnetic inclination I represents the angle formed between the total intensity $B _ { F }$ and the horizontal plane xoy. Based on the definitions of magnetic declination and magnetic inclination, the magnetic declination D and magnetic inclination I can determine a unique location. The specific calculations are as follows:

$$
\left\{ \begin{array}{l} D = \arctan (B _ {y} / B _ {x}) \\ I = \arctan (B _ {z} / \sqrt {B _ {x} ^ {2} + B _ {y} ^ {2}}). \end{array} \right. \tag {2}
$$
