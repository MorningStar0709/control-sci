$$x [ k ] = \left[ x _ {1} [ k ], x _ {2} [ k ] \right] ^ {\top} := \left[ \hat {G} [ k ], \tilde {\theta} [ k ] \right] ^ {\top}, \tag {32}$$

The discrete-time system (33) has a small parameter $\epsilon = h$ and the discrete-time vector field $f [ k , x , \epsilon ]$ is T -periodic in k such that

$$T = \frac {2 \pi}{\omega}. \tag {36}$$

Thus, the discrete-time averaging method [6], [7] can be applied to $f [ k , x , \epsilon ]$ in the limit ϵ  0.

The averaging approach allows one to characterize in what sense the behavior of a constructed averaged autonomous system approximates that of the original non-autonomous system (33). Intuitively, when the system evolves on a slower time scale than the excitation, its behavior is predominantly governed by the average effect of the excitation over one period.

By applying the discrete-time averaging technique to (33), we obtain the averaged system

$$x ^ {\mathrm{av}} [ k + 1 ] = x ^ {\mathrm{av}} [ k ] + \epsilon f ^ {\mathrm{av}} [ x ^ {\mathrm{av}} ], \tag {37}f ^ {\mathrm{av}} [ x ^ {\mathrm{av}} ] = \lim _ {\epsilon \rightarrow 0} \lim _ {T \rightarrow \infty} \frac {1}{T} \sum_ {k = s + 1} ^ {s + T} f [ k, x ^ {\mathrm{av}}, \epsilon ]. \tag {38}$$
