# Quantifying Trade-Offs Between Stability and Goal-Obfuscation∗

Yixuan Wang†, Dan P. Guralnik‡, and Warren E. Dixon

# ABSTRACT

Safety-critical autonomy in adversarial settings demands more than Lyapunov stability of tracking error signals. An agent executing a goal-directed trajectory is intrinsically legible to a passive observer running online Bayesian inference, because the contractive dynamics of any Lyapunov basin of attraction concentrates posterior belief over the latent intent parameters. This article initiates the study of intent privacy over a continuous state space as a joint control problem on the physical state combined with the latent belief state of a putative observer.

With the main challenges concentrated around the analysis of the belief-state dynamics, the agent dynamics is assumed to be simple, modeled by the differential inclusion x˙ ∈ u + ¯dB. That is, the agent is fully actuated with bounded unknown disturbance to the control input. The observer’s intent inference process is modeled as a discrete-time stochastic dynamical system evolving over the belief state space of a Rao–Blackwellized particle filter (RBPF) reasoning over large random samples of possible agent goals. The agent’s control input is modeled as a piecewise constant signal, with jumps matching the RBPF update times. Building on a prior intent-inference framework and its online-computable, KL-based information leakage measurement, a privacy constraint is imposed, which amounts to maintaining information leakage above a prescribed threshold with high probability, using probabilistic discrete-time control barrier functions (PCBFs). A key technical contribution is the derivation of separate PCBF results for the Bayesian update step and the resampling step of the RBPF, enabling a PCBF result for the full update as well as integration of the privacy constraint with the agent’s task-side tracking requirement. Finally, a joint feasibility analysis is carried out by examining the interplay between the privacy constraint and the tracking envelope.

# 1 Introduction

Control Lyapunov functions and control barrier functions provide a standard mechanism for synthesizing real-time safety critical controllers through online convex programs Ames et al. [2016, 2019]. In this paradigm, a controller is minimally perturbed at each timestep so that the resulting input simultaneously drives a Lyapunov function toward zero and keeps a barrier function nonnegative, yielding joint guarantees of stability and constraint satisfaction through a single quadratic program Ames et al. [2016]. The framework has been deployed across a wide range of domains including legged locomotion, adaptive cruise control, and multi-robot coordination Ames et al. [2019].

An implicit assumption underlying Lyapunov-based controller synthesis is that closed loop stability is decidedly a desirable property. Classically, the structural aim of Lyapunov-based design is to produce closed loop dynamics with normally hyperbolic invariant manifold (NHIM) structure Palmer [1995], in which the basin of attraction decomposes as a product of the goal-parameterized attractor with a normal fiber on which the dynamics acts contractively. Consequently, if the attractor structure is known to (or could be inferred by) an external observer up to a finite-dimensional parameter vector, the contractive geometry of the closed loop trajectories concentrates the observer’s posterior over those parameters with increasing speed as the agent converges to the goal. For a goal-directed agent, the relevant latent parameters are the goal location, goal radius, and desired arrival time, and this legibility problem is exacerbated by the fact that task optimal trajectories are precisely those most concentrated near the attracting manifold. The work of Dragan et al. [2013] showed that trajectories optimizing a task cost are inherently legible: a Bayesian observer can rapidly concentrate posterior belief over the agent’s goal from a short observation window, and intentional departure from task optimality is necessary to reduce this legibility. Complementary work treats the observer’s inference problem as inverse planning Ramírez and Geffner [2009], Baker et al. [2009] or maximum entropy inverse reinforcement learning Ziebart et al. [2008], confirming that goal-stabilizing trajectories are informationally richest in precisely the regimes where Lyapunov methods are most effective.

Prior work Wang et al. [2025b] formalized and quantified this legibility for continuous-state systems of the form studied here. There, the adversary models the agent’s intent and maintains $\dot { N } \gg 0$ weighted particles to recursively estimate the agent’s intent from noisy position measurements via a Rao-Blackwellized particle filter (RBPF), demonstrating that under nominal conditions the adversary rapidly recovers the agent’s true intent, underscoring the practical severity of the legibility problem for any agent operating under Lyapunov-based control.

The present paper addresses the control synthesis question left open by Wang et al. [2025b]: given a capable adversary running the RBPF inference framework, how should the agent select its control input to delay inference concentration while simultaneously satisfying task stability and tracking requirements?

Contributions. The contributions of this paper are as follows. First, the intent privacy task is formulated as a piecewise constant (intermittent actuation) control problem over the latent belief dynamics on the probability simplex induced by RBPF weight updates (extended by the $\mathrm { R B P F } \mathrm { s }$ state estimation variables), rather than as an auxiliary objective on the physical state.

Second, a finite horizon privacy-in-probability guarantee for the RBPF update cycle is derived. The technical challenge of analyzing the resampling step is overcome by observing that the RBPF update may be regarded as the composition of two probabilistically independent random transformations of the belief state space: the Bayesian update, and the resampling step. Probabilistic bounds on the increment in information leakage are derived for each of these transformations, and combined into a PCBF result for the overall RBPF update, following the PCBF framework developed in Mestres et al. [2025]. It is noteworthy that the bound for the resampling transformation is completely independent of the dynamics and control input, motivating future work on extensions to more complex dynamics of the agent.

Third, a joint feasibility analysis is carried out, examining the interplay between the privacy constraint and the tracking constraint via affine interpolation between two types of control input, one selected for obfuscation and the other for optimized tracking. The tensions between the constraints are revealed as inequality constraints on the interpolation parameter, which may be seen to be jointly feasible when the prescribed tracking error envelope is not overly restrictive. Further study of convergence properties of the RBPF is necessary to produce results establishing the infeasibility of obfuscation if practical stability of tracking errors is enforced.

# 2.1 Intent Inference

Intent Model. The agent’s intent is modeled as a triple $\theta ^ { * } \triangleq ( x ^ { * } , r ^ { * } , t ^ { * } )$ , constrained to a compact domain Θ, where $x ^ { * } \in \mathbb { R } ^ { n }$ is the goal center, $r ^ { * } > 0$ is the goal radius, and $t ^ { * } > 0$ is the desired arrival time; the corresponding goal set is the ball $x ^ { * } + { \bar { r } } ^ { * } \mathbb { B }$ , which the agent aims to reach by time $t ^ { * }$ . The adversary assumes that, if the (unknown) intent were θ, then the agent’s closed-loop motion is goal-stabilizing up to an unknown disturbance bounded in norm by a known ¯d:

$$
\dot {\hat {x}} \in f _ {\theta} (\hat {x}) + \bar {d} \mathbb {B}, \quad f _ {\theta} (\hat {x}) \triangleq - \lambda (\theta) (\hat {x} - x (\theta)),
$$

where λ(θ) ≜ max $\begin{array} { r } { \left\{ \frac { \hat { d } } { r ( \theta ) } , \frac { 1 } { t ( \theta ) } \log \frac { R } { r ( \theta ) } \right\} } \end{array}$ , ensuring that the agent will enter the goal ball $\boldsymbol { x } ^ { * } + \boldsymbol { r } ^ { * } \mathbb { B }$ by time $t ^ { * }$

RBPF State Representation. The adversary maintains N weighted particles $\{ ( \theta _ { k } ^ { ( i ) } , \omega _ { k } ^ { ( i ) } ) \} _ { i = 1 } ^ { N }$ to estimate $\theta ^ { * }$ . We distinguish three stages within each timestep k:

2. Pre-resampling weights $\omega _ { k } ^ { \sharp ( i ) } ;$ : weights after Bayesian update at timestep k, before resampling.   
3. Post resampling weights $\omega _ { k } ^ { ( i ) }$ : final weights at timestep k after resampling (if triggered).

Initialization. # 2.2 Probabilistic safety and PCBF

Consider a discrete-time controlled stochastic system

$$
x _ {t + 1} = F (x _ {t}, u _ {t}, d _ {t}), \quad t \in \mathbb {Z} _ {\geq 0},
$$

where $x _ { t } \in \mathcal { X } \subseteq \mathbb { R } ^ { n }$ is the state, $u _ { t } \in \mathcal { U } \subseteq \mathbb { R } ^ { m }$ is the control input constrained to an admissible set $u ,$ and $d _ { t } \in \mathcal { D }$ is an exogenous random disturbance. We assume $\{ d _ { t } \} _ { t \ge 0 }$ is an i.i.d. sequence with common distribution $\mathcal { P } _ { d }$ (equivalently, $d _ { t } \sim \bar { \mathcal { P } } _ { d } )$ . Unless stated otherwise, probabilities below are taken with respect to $\mathcal { P } _ { d }$ (and any additional exogenous randomness entering $F )$ , conditioned on the current $( x _ { t } , u _ { t } )$ .

Safe set and finite-horizon probabilistic safety. Let $h : \mathcal { X }  \mathbb { R }$ be a measurable function defining the safe set

$$
C := \{x \in \mathcal {X}: h (x) \geq 0 \}.
$$

Given a horizon $H \in \mathbb { N }$ and a failure tolerance $\varepsilon \in ( 0 , 1 )$ , finite-horizon probabilistic safety requires

$$
\mathbb {P} [ x _ {t} \in C | x _ {0} ] \geq 1 - \varepsilon \quad \text { for   all } t = 0, 1, \dots , H. \tag {6}
$$

One-step PCBF condition. Fix $\alpha \in [ 0 , 1 ]$ and $\delta \in ( 0 , 1 )$ . Following Mestres et al. [2025], the function h is called a δ-PCBF if for every $x \in C$ there exists an admissible input $u = u ( x ) \in \mathcal { U }$ such that

$$
\mathbb {P} \big [ h (F (x, u, d)) \geq \alpha h (x) | x, u ] \geq 1 - \delta , \tag {7}
$$

where the probability is taken over $d \sim \mathcal { P } _ { d } .$ . In online control, (7) is enforced at time t with the chosen input $u _ { t } \colon$

$$
\mathbb {P} [ h (x _ {t + 1}) \geq \alpha h (x _ {t}) | x _ {t}, u _ {t} ] \geq 1 - \delta . \tag {8}
$$

From one-step to horizon guarantee. Let $\pi : \mathcal { X }  \mathcal { U }$ be a feedback policy and set $u _ { t } = \pi ( x _ { t } )$ . If (8) holds for all $t = 0 , 1 , \ldots , H - 1$ whenever $x _ { t } \in C$ , then the probability of remaining in C for H steps is lower-bounded by

$$
\mathbb {P} \left[ x _ {t} \in C \mid x _ {0} \in C \right] \geq (1 - \delta) ^ {H} \text {   for   all   } t = 0, 1, \dots , H.
$$

Consequently, to satisfy (6) it suffices to choose δ such that $( 1 - \delta ) ^ { H } \geq 1 - \varepsilon ,$ i.e.,

$$
\delta \leq 1 - (1 - \varepsilon) ^ {1 / H}.
$$

# 3 Problem Formulation

We consider two principal entities, an adversary and an agent, whose positions evolve in $\mathbb { R } ^ { n } , n \in \{ 2 , 3 \}$ , corresponding to planar $( n = 2 )$ ) or spatial $( n = 3 )$ motion. The agent has the goal of reaching the ball $x ^ { * } + r ^ { * } \mathbb { B }$ within a time $t ^ { * }$ . The goal must be reached without disclosing the intent parameter $\bar { \theta } ^ { * }$ to the adversary, while maintaining a time-dependent bound $\varrho ( t )$ on the error $\| x ( t ) - x _ { d } ( t ) \|$ , where $x _ { d }$ is determined by $\theta ^ { * }$ and the initial position $x _ { 0 } \in \Omega$ of the agent. The adversary’s objective is to infer the agent’s intent parameter $\theta ^ { * }$ by observing the agent’s trajectory. The adversary is assumed to be a passive observer, focusing the analysis on the agent’s control decisions aimed at obscuring $\theta ^ { * }$ from the adversary. Note that the adversary is modeled internally by the agent solely as a proxy for intent-inference risk. Accordingly, we do not explicitly model the adversary’s observation sampling rate, nor do we address the question of how the agent would access or verify the adversary’s inferred belief in a real deployment.

For simplicity, the agent is assumed to be fully actuated, but subject to bounded disturbances,

$$
\dot {x} \in u + \bar {d} \mathbb {B}, \tag {9}
$$

where the state $\boldsymbol { x } \in \mathbb { R } ^ { n }$ is assumed to be measurable by the agent, $u \in \mathcal { U } \triangleq \mathbb { R } ^ { n }$ is the agent’s control input, and $\bar { d } > 0$ is a known constant. The task is encoded by the parameter $\theta ^ { * }$ , whose values are confined to the domain

$$
\Theta \triangleq R \mathbb {B} \times [ r _ {\min}, r _ {\max} ] \times [ T _ {\min}, T _ {\max} ],
$$

where $R > 0 , 0 < r _ { m i n } \ll r _ { m a x } \ll R ,$ and $0 < T _ { m i n } \ll T _ { m a x }$ are parameters of the problem, fixed in advance. The notation $\theta = ( x ( \theta ) , r ( \theta ) , t ( \theta ) )$ for $\theta \in \Theta$ will be used when there is no risk of ambiguity. For each $\theta \in \Theta$ and $q \in \mathbb { R } ^ { n }$ , a smooth reference path

$$
x _ {q, \theta}: [ 0, \infty) \to \mathbb {R} ^ {n}, x _ {q, \theta} (0) = q, x _ {q, \theta} (t (\theta)) = x (\theta),
$$

is available to the agent, whose task, given $x ( 0 ) = q { \mathrm { . } }$ , is to track this path by selecting control inputs $u ( x , t )$ such that the tracking error $e ( t ) \triangleq x ( t ) - x _ { q , \theta } ( t )$ satisfies

$$
\left\| e (t) \right\| \leq \varrho (t), \varrho (t (\theta)) = r (\theta) \tag {10}
$$

for some increasing function $\varrho : [ 0 , \infty )  [ 0 , \infty )$ . In an unobstructed (homogeneous and isotropic) environment, it is natural to select

$$
x _ {q, \theta} (t) := q + \frac {t}{t (\theta)} (x (\theta) - q) \tag {11}
$$

The agent is assumed to be aware of the presence of an adversary attempting to infer the intent parameter $\theta ^ { * }$ from its behavior. Therefore, in addition to (10), the agent is tasked with selecting a controller whose resulting trajectory maintains the adversary’s estimate $\hat { \theta }$ of the task parameter $\theta ^ { * }$ at an acceptable level of ambiguity within a time $T _ { a c c } < t ( \theta )$ .

# 4 Probabilistic Information Safety Bounds

This section applies the PCBF framework to the RBPF belief state update to obtain finite-horizon privacy-in-probability guarantees.

# 4.1 Information-state dynamics

The observation $y _ { k }$ received by the adversary at each timestep k is generated by the agent’s physical state, which is shaped by the control input u through the dynamics (9). Consequently, the agent’s choice of u influences the RBPF belief update through the induced observation distribution, motivating a formulation in which u appears explicitly as an argument of the belief transition. Define the information state (of the RBPF) as4

$$
z \triangleq (\boldsymbol {\theta}, \hat {\mathbf {x}}, \omega) \in \mathcal {Z},
$$

where $\pmb \theta = ( \theta ^ { ( i ) } ) _ { i \in \mathcal { P } }$ is the particle data, xˆ is the vector of state estimates associated with each particle, and ω is the RBPF weight vector. The information dynamics is represented in controlled stochastic form as

$$
z ^ {\prime} = F _ {I} (z, u, \eta), \tag {12}
$$

where $u \in \mathbb { R } ^ { n }$ is the agent’s control input, η aggregates all exogenous randomness driving the belief update including the observation noise $\xi$ and the resampling uncertainty ζ, and $z ^ { \prime } = ( \pmb { \theta } ^ { \prime } , \hat { \mathbf { x } } ^ { \prime } , \omega ^ { \prime } )$ denotes the successor information state. As long as the agent’s control input is decided independently of history, the following assumption is merited.

Assumption 1 (Controlled Markov Property). The successor information state $z ^ { \prime }$ is a random variable induced by (12), and the sequence $\{ \eta _ { k } = \eta _ { k } ( t _ { k } ) \} _ { k \geq 0 }$ is i.i.d.

Consequently, when evaluated along a trajectory $z _ { k } \triangleq z ( t _ { k } )$ at the k-th observation time, the sequence $\{ z _ { k } \} _ { k \ge 0 }$ is a controlled Markov process with transition kernel $\mathbb { P } [ z ^ { \prime } \in \cdot \mid \dot { z } , u ]$ .

Remark 2. The subscript k appears hereafter only when referring to the value of a quantity along a specific trajectory; all maps, barrier functions, and constraint sets considered in Sections 4 and 5 are time independent functions of z.

To perform the analysis, the transition is decomposed as

$$
F _ {I} (z, u, \eta) = \mathcal {R} (F ^ {\sharp} (z, u, \xi), \zeta),
$$

where $F ^ { \sharp }$ is the Bayesian update producing the pre-resampling information state, ξ is the observation noise, and ζ is the resampling noise. The intermediate state $\bar { F } ^ { \sharp } ( z , u , \xi )$ has the form

$$
z ^ {\sharp} \triangleq (\boldsymbol {\theta}, \hat {\mathbf {x}} ^ {\sharp}, \omega^ {\sharp}),
$$

noting that the Bayesian update of the RBPF has no effect on the intent parameters. The information leakage at state z is equal to

$$
H (z) \triangleq D _ {K L} \left(q _ {\theta^ {*}} \| \sum_ {i \in \mathcal {P}} \omega^ {(i)} q _ {\theta^ {(i)}}\right).
$$

It is helpful to define the quantities

$$
S _ {\nu} (z) \triangleq \sum_ {j \in \mathcal {P}} \omega^ {(j)} \gamma_ {\nu} (\theta^ {(j)}),
$$

where $\theta ^ { ( j ) }$ is the j-th particle held in the information state z. Note that $S _ { \nu } ( z )$ is a function of the information state alone. The post-resampling and pre-resampling information leakage lower bounds from Theorem 1 then take the time-independent forms

$$
H (z) \geq \underline {{H}} (z) \triangleq C - \sum_ {\nu \in \{x, r, t \}} \log S _ {\nu} (z),
$$

where $C \triangleq - \log \left( \sigma _ { x } ^ { 1 - n } \left( \frac { \mathtt { e } } { 2 } \right) ^ { \frac { n + 2 } { 2 } } \right)$ is a constant depending only on system parameters. Then, $\underline { { H } } ( z _ { k } ) \leq H _ { k } , \underline { { H } } ( z _ { k } ^ { \sharp } ) \leq$ $H _ { k } ^ { \sharp }$ hold pointwise along any trajectory. Similarly,

$$
H (z) = \int q _ {\theta^ {*}} \log \frac {q _ {\theta^ {*}}}{\sum_ {j} \omega^ {(j)} q ^ {(j)}} \leq \int q _ {\theta^ {*}} (- \sum_ {j} \omega^ {(j)} \log \frac {q ^ {(j)}}{q _ {\theta^ {*}}})
$$

$$
= \sum_ {j} \omega^ {(j)} \int q _ {\theta *} \log \frac {q _ {\theta *}}{q ^ {(j)}} = \sum_ {j} \omega^ {(j)} D _ {K L} \big (q _ {\theta *} \big \| q ^ {(j)} \big).
$$

Rewriting the right-hand side explicitly as

$$
H (z) \leq \underbrace {\sum_ {j \in \mathcal {P}} \omega^ {(j)} \underbrace {\sum_ {\nu \in \{x , r , t \}} \frac {\left\| \nu (\theta^ {*}) - \nu (\theta^ {(j)}) \right\| ^ {2}}{2 \sigma_ {\nu} ^ {2}}} _ {D _ {K L} \big (q _ {\theta^ {*}} \left\| q ^ {(j)}\right)} \leq M _ {H},} _ {\triangleq \bar {H} (z)}
$$

it follows that $\bar { H } ( z )$ is a continuous function of only the weights ω and the particles $\theta ^ { ( j ) } \in \Theta$ , all confined to compact sets. It follows that $\bar { H } ( z ) \leq M _ { H }$ for $M _ { H } > 0$ big enough.

# 4.2 Bayesian Update Barrier

Fixing $\gamma > 0$ , the information barrier with threshold $\gamma$ and the corresponding safe set are defined as

$$
b (z) \triangleq \underline {{H}} (z) - \gamma ,
$$

$$
C _ {I} \triangleq \{z \in \mathcal {Z}: b (z) \geq 0 \},
$$

where the threshold $\gamma$ must satisfy $\gamma \ll M _ { H }$ (to guarantee $b ( z ) > 0$ at initialization).

We establish a one step PCBF condition for the Bayesian update map $F ^ { \sharp }$ . The barrier change is

$$
b (z ^ {\sharp}) - b (z) = \sum_ {\nu \in \{x, r, t \}} \log \frac {S _ {\nu} (z)}{S _ {\nu} (z ^ {\sharp})}. \tag {13}
$$

Define the likelihood ratio

$$
r ^ {(j)} (y) \triangleq p (y | \hat {x} ^ {(j)}) / \sum_ {i} \omega^ {(i)} p (y | \hat {x} ^ {(i)}).
$$

Note that $\omega ^ { \sharp ( j ) } ~ = ~ \omega ^ { ( j ) } r ^ { ( j ) }$ and $\begin{array} { r } { \sum _ { j } \omega ^ { ( j ) } r ^ { ( j ) } ~ = ~ 1 } \end{array}$ , by (1). Then, the ratio $S _ { \nu } ( z ^ { \sharp } ) / S _ { \nu } ( z )$ lies in the interval $[ \operatorname* { m i n } _ { j } r ^ { ( j ) }$ , maxj r(j)] as a convex combination of the $r ^ { ( j ) }$ . It follows from (13) by summing over $\nu \in \{ x , r , t \}$ that

$$
\left| b (z ^ {\sharp}) - b (z) \right| \leq 3 B (z, y), \tag {14}
$$

where the bound $B ( z , y )$ is defined as

$$
B (z, y) \triangleq \max \bigl (\left| \log \max _ {j} r ^ {(j)} (y) \right|, \left| \log \min _ {j} r ^ {(j)} (y) \right| \bigr).
$$

To bound $B ( z , y )$ as a function of y, we compute $\nabla _ { y } \log { r ^ { ( j ) } ( y ) }$ . Since $p \big ( y | \hat { x } ^ { ( j ) } \big ) \sim \mathcal { N } \big ( \hat { x } ^ { ( j ) } , \Delta \big )$ by (1), a direct computation gives

$$
\nabla_ {y} \log r ^ {(j)} (y) = \boldsymbol {\Delta} ^ {- 1} \big (\hat {x} ^ {(j)} - \mu^ {\sharp} (y) \big),
$$

where $\begin{array} { r } { \mu ^ { \sharp } ( y ) \triangleq \sum _ { i } \omega ^ { \sharp ( i ) } ( y ) \hat { x } ^ { ( i ) } } \end{array}$ is the posterior weighted mean. Since $\mu ^ { \sharp } ( y ) \in \mathsf { c o n v } \big ( \{ \hat { x } ^ { ( i ) } \} _ { i = 1 } ^ { N } \big )$ for every $y ,$ defining the particle cloud diameter $D ( z ) \triangleq \mathrm { d i a m } \big ( \{ \hat { x } ^ { ( i ) } \} _ { i = 1 } ^ { N } \big )$ , the gradient is uniformly bounded for every y and $j \colon$

$$
\left\| \nabla_ {y} \log r ^ {(j)} (y) \right\| \leq \left\| \boldsymbol {\Delta} ^ {- 1} \right\| D (z). \tag {15}
$$

Denote this Lipschitz bound by $L ( z ) \triangleq \| \Delta ^ { - 1 } \| D ( z )$ and consider the function

$$
\psi (z) \triangleq B (z, \bar {y} (z)) \geq 0,
$$

where

$$
\bar {y} (z) \triangleq \arg \min _ {y} \max _ {j} \left\| y - \hat {x} ^ {(j)} \right\|
$$

is the Chebyshev center [Boyd and Vandenberghe, 2004, Chapter 8.5.1] of the particle cloud.

Lemma 1. For any $\delta _ { 1 } , \mu \in ( 0 , 1 )$ , define

$$
\Delta_ {b} = \Delta_ {b} (\delta_ {1}, \mu) \triangleq \mu A _ {1} + (1 - \mu) B _ {1},
$$

where

$$
A _ {1} \triangleq \sup _ {z \in \mathcal {Z}} 3 \psi (z) + 3 L (z) \left(\bar {d} \Delta t + \kappa_ {n} (\delta_ {1})\right)
$$

$$
B _ {1} \triangleq \sup _ {z \in \mathcal {Z}} 3 L (z) \big \| x _ {q, \theta^ {*}} (t _ {k + 1}) - \bar {y} (z) \big \|
$$

$$
\kappa_ {n} (\delta_ {1}) \triangleq \sqrt {\left\| \boldsymbol {\Delta} \right\| (n + 2 \sqrt {n \log \frac {1}{\delta_ {1}}} - 2 \log \delta_ {1})}
$$

Then for every $z \in { \mathcal { Z } }$ , there exists $u \in \mathbb { R } ^ { n }$ such that

$$
\mathbb {P} \left[ b (z ^ {\sharp}) \geq b (z) - \Delta_ {b} | z, u, x _ {k} \right] \geq 1 - \delta_ {1}.
$$

Proof. Let $\mu \in ( 0 , 1 )$ . Select $u _ { p }$ to be the privacy controller such that $x _ { k } + \Delta t u _ { p } = \bar { y } ( z )$ . Then the controller $u _ { b }$ for maintain privacy while following the reference path is defined $\mathrm { a s } ^ { 5 }$

$$
u _ {b} \triangleq \mu u _ {p} + (1 - \mu) (x _ {q, \theta^ {*}} (t _ {k + 1}) - x _ {k}).
$$

Under the agent dynamics (9), we have

$$
\left\| x _ {k + 1} - \mu \bar {y} - (1 - \mu) x _ {q, \theta^ {*}} (t _ {k + 1}) \right\| \leq \bar {d} \Delta t
$$

Then, the distance between the observation and the Chebyshev center satisfies

$$
\left\| y - \bar {y} (z) \right\| \leq (1 - \mu) \left\| x _ {q, \theta^ {*}} \left(t _ {k + 1}\right) - \bar {y} \right\| + \bar {d} \Delta t + \| \xi \|.
$$

By (14) and the Lipschitz bound (15),

$$
\left. \left| b \left(z ^ {\sharp}\right) - b (z) \right| \leq 3 \left[ \psi (z) + L (z) \| y - \bar {y} (z) \| \right] \right..
$$

By a computation following [Laurent and Massart, 2000, Lemma 1], one has

$$
\left\| \xi \right\| \leq \sqrt {\left\| \boldsymbol {\Delta} \right\| (n + 2 \sqrt {n \log \frac {1}{\delta_ {1}}} - 2 \log (\delta_ {1}))} = \kappa_ {n} (\delta_ {1})
$$

with probability at least $1 - \delta _ { 1 }$ . It follows that $| b ( z ^ { \sharp } ) - b ( z ) | \leq \Delta _ { b } .$ , hence $b ( z ^ { \sharp } ) \geq b ( z ) - \Delta _ { b }$ .

# 4.3 Resampling Update Barrier

The resampling step in the RBPF update (Section 2.1) introduces a discontinuous change in the empirical posterior when $z ^ { \sharp } \in { \mathcal { Z } }$ satisfies $N _ { e f f } = N _ { e f f } ( \bar { z } ^ { \sharp } ) \leq N _ { 0 }$ , recall (2). Denote the set of all such $z ^ { \sharp }$ by $\mathcal { Z } _ { 0 }$ .

Particles outside of ${ \mathcal { M } } ^ { \sharp } \subseteq { \mathcal { P } }$ , the set of $N _ { e f f }$ highest weight particles, are discarded. The rest are retained in the filter and replicated with weights $\textstyle { \frac { 1 } { N } }$ , each in $\bar { N _ { a } } = \bar { \lfloor \omega ^ { \sharp ( a ) } N / \omega _ { e f f . } ^ { \sharp } }$ ⌋ copies, where $\begin{array} { r } { \omega _ { e f f } ^ { \sharp } = \sum _ { a \in { \mathcal { M } } ^ { \sharp } } \omega ^ { \sharp ( a ) } } \end{array}$ . Let M denote the set of indices of replicated effective particles. M has cardinality $\textstyle \sum _ { a \in { \mathcal { M } } ^ { \sharp } } N _ { a }$ . The remaining particles are reinitialized by sampling from a distribution π over Θ. After resampling, all weights are assigned uniformly, $\begin{array} { r } { \omega ^ { ( i ) } = \frac { 1 } { N } } \end{array}$ for all $i \in \mathcal { P }$ . If no resampling is triggered $( N _ { e f f } > N _ { 0 } )$ , then set $\mathcal { M } : = \mathcal { M } ^ { \sharp }$ and $\mathbf { \bar { \boldsymbol { \omega } } } ^ { ( i ) } : = \boldsymbol { \omega } ^ { \sharp ( i ) }$ for all $i \in \mathcal { P }$ .

Recalling (5), let $\mathcal { M } ^ { \mathfrak { c } } = \mathcal { P } \setminus \mathcal { M }$ , with $N ^ { \prime } \triangleq | { \mathcal { M } } ^ { \mathfrak { c } } | \leq N _ { 0 }$ . The post resampling distribution $\hat { q }$ can be written as:

$$
\hat {q} \triangleq \frac {1}{N} \sum_ {a \in \mathcal {M}} q ^ {(a)} + \frac {1}{N} \sum_ {b \in \mathcal {M} ^ {\complement}} q ^ {(b)}.
$$

Similarly to (13), a probabilistic bound on the barrier value difference

$$
b (z ^ {\prime}) - b (z ^ {\sharp}) = \sum_ {\nu \in \{x, r, t \}} \log \frac {S _ {\nu} (z ^ {\sharp})}{S _ {\nu} (z ^ {\prime})} \tag {17}
$$

is being sought. One has:

$$
N \cdot S _ {\nu} (z ^ {\prime}) = \underbrace {\sum_ {a \in \mathcal {M}} \gamma_ {\nu} (\theta^ {(a)})} _ {\triangleq A _ {\nu}} + \underbrace {\sum_ {b \in \mathcal {M} ^ {\complement}} \gamma_ {\nu} (\theta^ {(b)})} _ {\triangleq Y _ {\nu}}.
$$

Note that $A _ { \nu }$ is a deterministic function of $z ^ { \sharp } ,$ , while $Y _ { \nu }$ a random variable. Define $\begin{array} { r } { \bar { Y } _ { \nu } \triangleq \frac { 1 } { N _ { 0 } } Y _ { \nu } \leq \frac { 1 } { N ^ { \prime } } Y _ { \nu } \in ( 0 , 1 ) } \end{array}$ .

Lemma 2 (Resampling barrier preservation). For any $\delta _ { 2 } \in ( 0 , 1 )$ , let $\begin{array} { r } { \epsilon = \epsilon ( \delta _ { 2 } ) \triangleq \sqrt { \frac { 1 } { 2 N _ { 0 } } \log \frac { 3 } { \delta _ { 2 } } } } \end{array}$ and define

$$
\Delta_ {r} \triangleq \sup _ {z ^ {\sharp} \in \mathcal {Z} _ {0}} \sum_ {\nu \in \{x, r, t \}} \log \Bigl (\frac {A _ {\nu} + N _ {0} \bigl (\mathbb {E} _ {\boldsymbol {\pi}} (\bar {Y} _ {\nu}) + \epsilon \bigr)}{N S _ {\nu} (z ^ {\sharp})} \Bigr).
$$

Note that $\Delta _ { r }$ depends on $\delta _ { 2 }$ . Then, for any $z ^ { \sharp } \in { \mathcal { Z } } _ { 0 }$ ,

$$
\mathbb {P} \left[ b (z ^ {\prime}) \geq b (z ^ {\sharp}) - \Delta_ {r} \mid z ^ {\sharp} \right] \geq 1 - \delta_ {2}
$$

holds for the post-resampling state $z ^ { \prime } = R ( z ^ { \sharp } , \zeta )$ .

Proof. $\operatorname { I f } z ^ { \sharp } \in { \mathcal { Z } }$ is such that $N _ { e f f } ( z ^ { \sharp } ) \leq N _ { 0 }$ , then the Höffding inequality yields

$$
\mathbb {P} \left[ \bar {Y} _ {\nu} \geq \mathbb {E} _ {\boldsymbol {\pi}} (\bar {Y} _ {\nu}) + \epsilon | z ^ {\sharp} \right] \leq \exp (- 2 N _ {0} \epsilon^ {2}) = \frac {\delta_ {2}}{3}
$$

Then, by the independence in (3), the event

$$
\mathcal {E} \triangleq \bigcap_ {\nu \in \{x, r, t \}} \{\bar {Y} _ {\nu} \leq \mathbb {E} _ {\boldsymbol {\pi}} (\bar {Y} _ {\nu}) + \epsilon) \}
$$

satisfies $\begin{array} { r } { \mathbb { P } [ \mathcal { E } ] \ge ( 1 - \frac { \delta _ { 2 } } { 3 } ) ^ { 3 } \ge 1 - 3 \frac { \delta _ { 2 } } { 3 } } \end{array}$ . Since log is monotone increasing and $N ^ { \prime } \leq N _ { 0 }$ , the equality (17) gives rise to

$$
\begin{array}{l} b (z ^ {\prime}) - b (z ^ {\sharp}) = \sum_ {\nu} \log \left(\frac {N S _ {\nu} (z ^ {\sharp})}{A _ {\nu} + N _ {0} \bar {Y} _ {\nu}}\right) \\ \geq \sum_ {\nu} \log \left(\frac {N S _ {\nu} (z ^ {\sharp})}{A _ {\nu} + N _ {0} (\mathbb {E} _ {\boldsymbol {\pi}} (\bar {Y} _ {\nu}) + \epsilon)}\right) \\ \end{array}
$$

being valid over the event E . This proves the Lemma.

# 4.4 Composite PCBF Bound

Both Lemma 1 and Lemma 2 establish additive lower bounds on the barrier change (13) with high probability, rather than on the multiplicative form required by the PCBF condition (7). The multiplicative form is recovered by restricting to the sublevel set

$$
C _ {I} ^ {\beta} \triangleq \{z \in \mathcal {Z}: b (z) \geq \beta \}.
$$

Indeed, for any $\Delta \in ( 0 , \beta )$ and $z \in C _ { I } ^ { \beta }$ one has

$$
b (z) - \Delta = b (z) \left(1 - \frac {\Delta}{b (z)}\right) \geq b (z) \left(1 - \frac {\Delta}{\beta}\right) = \alpha b (z), \tag {18}
$$

where $\alpha \triangleq 1 - \frac { \Delta } { \beta } \in ( 0 , 1 )$ . The following theorem applies this conversion to compose the two additive bounds into a single PCBF condition.

Theorem 2 (Composite information PCBF). Let $\delta _ { 1 } , \delta _ { 2 } \in ( 0 , 1 )$ and define $\delta _ { f } \triangleq 1 - ( 1 - \delta _ { 1 } ) ( 1 - \delta _ { 2 } )$ . Let $\Delta _ { t o t } \triangleq$ $\Delta _ { b } + \Delta _ { r }$ and let $\beta > \Delta _ { t o t }$ . Define $\begin{array} { r } { \alpha \triangleq 1 - \frac { \Delta _ { t o t } } { \beta } \in ( 0 , 1 ) } \end{array}$ . Then for every $z \in C _ { I } ^ { \beta }$ , there exists $u \in \mathbb { R } ^ { n }$ such that

$$
\mathbb {P} [ b (F _ {I} (z, u, \eta)) \geq \alpha b (z) | z, u ] \geq 1 - \delta_ {f}.
$$

Proof. Fix $z \in C _ { I } ^ { \beta } , \mathrm { s o } b ( z ) \ge \beta$ . Let u be the control whose existence is guaranteed by Lemma 1. Define the events

$$
E _ {1} \triangleq \left\{b (F ^ {\sharp} (z, u, \xi)) \geq b (z) - \Delta_ {b} \right\},
$$

$$
E _ {2} \triangleq \left\{b (R (z ^ {\sharp}, \zeta)) \geq b (z ^ {\sharp}) - \Delta_ {r} \right\}.
$$

By Lemma 1, $\mathbb { P } [ E _ { 1 } \mid z , u ] \ge 1 - \delta _ { 1 }$ 1. By Lemma 2, $\mathbb { P } [ E _ { 2 } \mid z ^ { \sharp } ] \ge 1 - \delta _ { 2 }$ . Since ξ and ζ are independent,

$$
\mathbb {P} \left[ E _ {1} \cap E _ {2} \mid z, u \right] \geq (1 - \delta_ {1}) (1 - \delta_ {2}) = 1 - \delta_ {f}.
$$

On the event $E _ { 1 } \cap E _ { 2 }$ , the chain of inequalities

$$
b \left(F _ {I} (z, u, \eta)\right) = b \left(R \left(z ^ {\sharp}, \zeta\right)\right) \geq b \left(z ^ {\sharp}\right) - \Delta_ {r} \geq b (z) - \Delta_ {t o t}
$$

holds. Since $b ( z ) \geq \beta > \Delta _ { t o t }$ , applying (18) completes the proof.

Remark 4 (Separation of privacy and tracking constraints). Theorem 2 establishes the existence of a feasible input $u \in U$ satisfying the PCBF condition without reference to the tracking envelope ϱ. When both constraints can be simultaneously satisfied, the feasible controller set is nonempty and the agent can maintain privacy while tracking. When the tracking envelope becomes too restrictive, the feasible set of the joint program may be empty.

# 5 Discussion: Joint Feasibility

Theorem 2 guarantees that for every $z \in C _ { I } ^ { \beta } , \beta > \Delta _ { t o t } .$ , at each time $t _ { k }$ there exists at least one control input satisfying the PCBF privacy condition. However, according to Remark 3, the success of this control input in maintaining the tracking envelope constraint is dependent on our ability to select $\mu$ at time k according to (16).

The requirement that $\beta > \Delta _ { t o t }$ represents an additional tension between the problem constraints. On one hand, $\beta$ is provided, essentially, by the initial condition, as it must be a lower bound on $\dot { D } _ { K L } \big ( q _ { \theta ^ { * } } \big | \big | \hat { q } _ { 0 } \big )$ (the agent is initialized in $C _ { I } ^ { \beta } )$ . On the other hand, at the times $t _ { k }$ one has

$$
\Delta_ {t o t} = \Delta_ {r} + \Delta_ {b} = \Delta_ {r} + \mu A _ {1} + (1 - \mu) B _ {1},
$$

where $\mu$ is allowed to depend on $k ,$ and influences the control input being selected. Thus $\beta$ must satisfy

$$
\beta > \min \{A _ {1}, B _ {1} \} + \Delta_ {r}, \tag {19}
$$

or the joint PCBF condition will fail for any sequence of choices of the $\mu$ values. Next, given $\beta$ which satisfies (19), it is necessary to select $\mu$ to guarantee $\begin{array} { r } { \Delta _ { t o t } < \beta . } \end{array}$ . Equivalently,

$$
\beta - \Delta_ {r} - B _ {1} > \mu (A _ {1} - B _ {1}).
$$

If $A _ { 1 } - B _ { 1 } \ge 0$ at time k, the resulting upper bound combines with (16) to produce a value for $\mu$ (and through it, a control input maintaining both mission constraints); if $A _ { 1 } - B _ { 1 } < 0$ results in a positive lower bound on $\mu ,$ which may or may not be consistent with (16). However, it follows from (16), that if ϱ grows fast enough, no restriction is imposed on $\mu$ that would conflict with this lower bound.

# 6 Conclusion

This paper addresses the problem of intent privacy for a goal-directed agent operating in the presence of a passive adversary running an online Bayesian inference algorithm to infer the agent’s goal. Rather than modify the task or treat privacy as an auxiliary penalty, intent privacy was formulated as a control problem with piecewise-constant actuation over a state space extended by the latent belief state, while treating the RBPF information state ${ z } = ( \theta , \hat { x } , \omega )$ as a controlled Markov process and the observer’s KL-based information leakage as the quantity to be regulated.

The main technical contribution is a composite probabilistic control barrier function result that provides finite-horizon privacy-in-probability guarantees for the full RBPF update cycle. The Bayesian update step produces an additive lower bound on the barrier increment by controlling the observation y toward the Chebyshev center of the particle cloud. The resampling step produces a second additive lower bound by applying the Höffding’s inequality to the random contribution of reinitialized particles. The two bounds compose multiplicatively through a union bound argument, yielding a single PCBF condition covering the entire update cycle with failure probability $\delta _ { f }$ .

A complementary contribution exposes tensions between the obfuscation component of the mission and its tracking component. It is demonstrated that large uncertainties of the RBPF representation of the the agent’s state can render the joint mission infeasible in the presence of tight tracking envelope constraints. At the same time, feasibility is recovered if the envelope constraints are relaxed, and the relation is quantified.

Several directions remain open for future investigation, not least among them is obtaining convergence results which characterize the convergence (with rates) of the RBPF intent-filtering framework. Such results are inextricably linked to the joint feasibility of the tracking and obfuscation problems (see Lemma 1), especially in relation to establishing an understanding of the natural growth rates of tracking envelopes permissible for effective obfuscation. Further work developing numerical and hardware experiments is necessary for validation.
